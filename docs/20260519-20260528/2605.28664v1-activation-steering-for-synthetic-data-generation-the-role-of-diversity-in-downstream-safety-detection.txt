## Abstract 

Safety detection models require examples of HHH (Helpful, Harmless, Honest)-violating outputs for robust generalization, however such examples are scarce. Activation Steering (AS) has emerged as a data-efficient method for generating target-concept-aligned responses. We investigate whether AS can generate high-quality training datasets for downstream classifiers, a question that remains untested. We present a two-fold study with intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation across 4 concepts 

√ó 2 models √ó 4 steering methods. Intrinsically , beyond the field-standard rubric of steering success (concept alignment) and coherence, we introduce sample- and set-level diversity as a quality axis previously absent from the literature, and find that increasing steering strength reduces response diversity. Extrinsically , we replace 

HHH-violating examples in the available training data with steered generations and fine-tune detection classifiers. AS-generated data results in a better classifier than the prompting-generated data on 3 of 4 concepts. However, only 41 of 136 AS configurations outperform prompting, indicating that downstream utility lies in a narrow regime that jointly satisfies success, coherence, and diversity. The harmonic mean of these three axes correlates with downstream AUROC more consistently across concepts than success and coherence alone, providing a practical heuristic target for practitioners tuning AS hyperparameters. Together, our results highlight the potential of AS in synthetic data generation for improving safety detection and identify diversity as a critical, previously overlooked axis for tuning AS. 

## 1 Introduction 

As large language models (LLMs) continue to gain widespread adoption, ensuring their alignment with the principles of Helpfulness, Harmlessness, and Honesty (HHH) has become paramount [ 2 ]. A central component of this safety infrastructure is the development of robust detection models that flag deviations from these principles, such as toxic outputs, hallucinations, or sycophancy [ 10 , 18 , 20 , 28 , 29 , 32 , 40 , 45 ]. However, the success of safety training creates a paradox: as models become safer through Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), naturally occurring instances of HHH-violating behavior become statistically sparse [ 21 ]. This class imbalance is a significant bottleneck for training the next generation of safety classifiers, which require diverse, high-quality rare-class examples (i.e., HHH-violating responses) to generalize effectively. One avenue for synthesising such rare-class examples is jailbreaking or red-teaming. However, these methods operate in the discrete token space, relying on manual or automated adversarial

Activation steering (AS) offers a complementary alternative that operates within this internal representation space [ 37 , 50 , 19 , 3]: a small steering vector added to the model‚Äôs activations nudges generation toward a target concept, i.e., alignment. Crucially, prior work shows that only a handful of contrastive examples suffice to estimate an effective steering vector [ 42 , 8, 39 ], making the method appealing for the data-scarce regime characteristic of HHH-violating outputs. This raises a natural question: How effective is AS in generating valuable training data for safety detection models? 

Answering this requires evaluating steering more rigorously than current practices. Prior work assesses steered generation in two regimes: short generations (MC-QA) via accuracy [ 49 , 55 ], and long generations via LLM-as-a-Judge along two axes: steering success and coherence [ 50 , 41 , 5, 55 ]. We target the more demanding long-form regime and argue that this two-axis evaluation is insufficient when the downstream goal is producing classifier training data. We therefore strengthen evaluation along three dimensions. (i) Horizon: most prior studies cap generation at <128 tokens [ 50 , 41 , 5, 55 ,12 , 24 ]; we evaluate at 256 tokens, exposing failure modes that only emerge over longer spans. (ii) Diversity: motivated by diversity collapse under alignment [ 23 , 54 , 13 ] and the role of diversity in continual training [ 43 , 46 , 16 ], we measure both sample- and set-level diversity, alongside surface statistics such as response length, since detectors trained on distributionally distorted data may fail to generalise. (iii) Downstream utility: we report post-fine-tuning detector AUROC, a direct extrinsic test of whether steered data is genuinely useful. To our knowledge, our work is the first to evaluate activation steering with set-level metrics and downstream classifier performance. With this strengthened setup, we run controlled experiments on the role of diversity and two key design choices: model scale and all- vs. one-layer steering. We focus on model scale because existing evidence on model scale is contradictory across prior work [ 5, 49 , 12 ]. For steering strategy, early methods proposed and used a one-layer setup [ 50 ]. However, recent work shows all-layer steering yields better concept alignment with substantially less hyperparameter tuning burden 2 [5]. To understand the role of diversity and the key design choices, we set up our experiments across four HHH-violating concepts that capture principal failure modes of modern LLMs: unfaithfulness (RAGTruth) [ 35 ], toxicity [ 15 ], hallucination [ 41 ], and sycophancy [ 41 ]. We extract steering vectors with four methods: Contrastive Activation Addition (CAA) [ 41 ], a normalised CAA variant, Recursive Feature Machines (RFM) [ 5 ], and Logistic Regression. ## 2 Related Works 

Steering Methods. Activation steering methods differ along three axes. (i) Contrastive pair construction: pairs are curated either from natural data [ 41 ] or generated by an LLM [ 5 ]; while many studies argue a handful of pairs suffice [ 42 , 8, 39 ], others use thousands [ 7, 24 ]. (ii) Vector extraction: the dominant approach is the mean-difference of paired activations [ 41 , 50 ]; alternatives include PCA over difference vectors [ 58 ], sparse autoencoder features [ 9, 17 , 55 ], supervised linear probes [ 25 , 5 ], Recursive Feature Machines [ 5 ], and preference-optimization-based extraction [ 7]. 

(iii) Test-time intervention: LLM hidden states can be modified along four axes‚ÄĒ where in LLM? ,

which examples? , which tokens? , and which hidden dimensions? . For ‚Äėwhere in LLM?‚Äô , most studies intervene on the residual stream [ 41 , 7, 58 , 5 , 50 ], with others targeting self-attention [ 25 , 11 ] or post-LM-head representations [ 12 ]. In addition, two distinct steering strategies, all-layers [ 5, 7 ] and one-layer [ 41 , 50 ], have been explored in the literature. Adaptive methods select ‚Äėwhich examples?‚Äô 

to steer or adjust steering strength per example [ 24 , 53 , 48 , 47 ]. For ‚Äėwhich tokens?‚Äô decision, last-k

[58 ] and all-token [ 41 ] steering dominate, while a few works favor steering on targeted tokens [ 34 ]. Steering all hidden dimensions is standard for which hidden dimensions? , with selective-dimension variants as exceptions [ 25 , 4, 52 ]. The dominant steering operator is scaled addition, sometimes combined with normalization for better control of activation distribution [33, 47]. 

Evaluation of Steered Responses. Steered responses are evaluated predominantly along two dimensions: steering success and text coherence (also referred to as fluency or quality). For open-ended generation, both are scored by an LLM-Judge [ 41 , 5, 50 , 55 ]; for multiple-choice setups, steering success reduces to text- or logit-based accuracy on the target option [ 49 , 55 ]. Task-specific variations of steering success also appear in applications such as refusal [ 24 ] and instruction-following [47 ]. Across methods, a consistent trade-off emerges between steering success and general capabilities [ 3, 52 , 41 ]. A more rigorous evaluation reveals that many concepts are not steerable, and even within steerable concepts, a substantial fraction of examples can exhibit anti-steering properties [ 49 ]. With the reported high variance in steering effectiveness across concepts, examples, models, and methods [49 , 12 ], a priori prediction of the value of steered generations is challenging. We address this gap by introducing a validation heuristic that correlates with downstream benefits. 

## 3 Preliminaries 

Activation steering rests on the linear representation hypothesis: high-level concepts such as honesty or toxicity correspond to approximately linear directions in the latent activation space of large language models (LLMs) [ 37 ]. The framework comprises two stages. First, a steering vector v ‚ąą Rd,i.e., target concept direction, is estimated using a paired dataset D of contrastive examples and an extraction method f : D ‚Üí v. Second, at inference time, the LLM‚Äôs hidden states ( h) are modified through an intervention h ‚Üź g(h, v) to align LLM generations with the target concept. Activation steering methods therefore differ along three axes: the construction of D, the extraction map f (¬∑), and the intervention g(¬∑). To isolate the effect of the extraction function, we fix D across models and methods, and fix the intervention to scaled addition, g(h, v; őĽ) = h + őĽv, where the scalar őĽ controls steering strength and is a hyperparameter. A positive őĽ steers the model toward the target concept, while a negative őĽ steers it away. ## 4 Experimental Setup 

We assess the utility of activation steering as a data-generation tool for safety-detection models. The pipeline has three stages: (i) curate contrastive pairs for each target concept, (ii) extract steering vectors per (concept, method, model, layer) combination, and (iii) use the steered generations in two downstream investigations. First, an intrinsic analysis (design-choice analysis) and second an 

extrinsic analysis (safety-classifier fine-tuning). The following subsections detail stages (i) and (ii); the downstream investigations are described in Sections 4.3 and 4.4.

We study four HHH-relevant concepts, each drawn from a public dataset: unfaithfulness from RAGTruth [ 35 ], toxicity from RealToxicityPrompts [ 15 ], and hallucination and sycophancy from CAAData [ 41 ]. Fine-tuning and test data. Once hyperparameters are tuned, we use the configured steering vector (or prompting baseline) to generate synthetic HHH-violating examples for the downstream classifier (Mistral-7B [22]). The official test split of each dataset is used as-is to evaluate classifiers trained with either the original, prompting-based, or steered data. The labels for the test examples are formed based on the same rule used to create the contrastive pairs. We describe the per-dataset criteria in the following paragraph.

With contrastive datasets curated and steering vectors estimated, we now generate steered responses and assess them along multiple axes. The main design choices under study are model scale, steering scale ( őĽ), and layer coverage (one vs. all); the remaining hyperparameters are fixed as in Table 3. Generation protocol. A full sweep over 4 concepts √ó 4 steering methods √ó 2 models √ó 10 values of 

őĽ yields 320 generated datasets. To keep the compute budget tractable while preserving statistical power, we randomly sample 100 unique prompts from each concept‚Äôs validation split and generate steered responses at 256 tokens for every configuration (refer to Table 3 for generation configuration). Because all design-choice tuning is performed on this held-out validation split, downstream results in Section 4.4 remain free of selection bias from this stage. 

Evaluation metrics. We evaluate generations along three axes: 

Steering success. Following standard practice [ 50 , 41 , 55 ], we use LLM-as-a-Judge (Haiku 4.53) to assess the extent to which a response exhibits the target concept with a Likert scale of 1-5.

Coherence. We score each response with the reward model ArmoRM [ 51 ], which exposes coherence as a dedicated head rather than collapsing it into a single quality scalar. This separation is essential for our setting: scalar reward models implicitly penalise HHH-violating content, so a response that is unfaithful-but-fluent would receive a low score for the wrong reason. ArmoRM also provides a substantially cheaper alternative to API-based judges, given the scale of the sweep.

## 5 Results 

5.1 Steering degrades diversity 

We begin with the simplest question: how does each evaluation metric move with steering scale? Figure 2 reports the average Pearson correlation between steering scale ( őĽ) and five metrics across all (model, dataset) combinations: steering success (LLM-Judge), coherence (reward model), sample-level diversity (MTLD-MB), set-level diversity ( 1/compression ratio), and response length. All individual values are listed in Table 4. Two findings replicate prior work [ 55 , 56 ]: stronger steering correlates with better alignment and with worse coherence. We then extend the analysis to diversity. Sample-level diversity (MTLD-MB) decreases with increasing őĽ in every (model, dataset) cell except OLMo-32B on Toxicity, the concept that is also hardest to steer (lowest success correlation, 0.12 /0.14 ). For Unfaithfulness and Toxicity, the MTLD-MB correlations sit near zero, indicating almost no sample-level degradation. Set-level diversity tells a sharper story: the compression-ratio metric is strongly negative in 7/8 cells (down to ‚ąí0.93 ), with the lone exception being Sycophancy on OLMo-7B ( +0 .73 ). Set-level diversity therefore degrades more severely than sample-level diversity. This parallels a known pattern in the LLM alignment literature, where post-training collapses the collective diversity of model outputs [ 23 , 54 , 14 ] while maintaining sample-level diversity [ 6 ]. Because steering acts as a data-efficient alignment mechanism, similar distributional contraction may underlie both, though we leave this question to future work. Finally, response length correlates with őĽ in directions that depend on model size: mostly negative for OLMo-7B and consistently positive for OLMo-32B. A steered dataset can therefore drift away from the natural length distribution in either direction, depending on the model, and introduces an additional axis of variability to control when steering for downstream use.

5.3 Steered data improves downstream detection 

We now examine the downstream utility of steered data: when steered generations replace the HHH-violating examples in the training set, how closely can a fine-tuned safety detector match training on the original data? Guided by Section 5.2, we restrict the sweep to OLMo-7B and to 

őĽ ‚ąą { 0.4, 0.5, . . . , 1.0}, dropping smaller steering scales since they yield generations close to the unsteered model. Crossing 4 concepts, 4 methods, and 7 scales gives 112 steered fine-tuning runs, compared against 8 baselines (one original-data and one prompting-only run per concept). The replace-rather-than-augment design isolates the contribution of the synthetic positives, since both compared datasets share the same HHH-compliant pool. We therefore do not expect to exceed the original-data ceiling; we measure how closely each synthetic source approaches it. Figure 4 reports detection AUC per configuration. Per-concept behaviour varies. Hallucination saturates near the original-data ceiling already at low őĽ, leaving little room for steering to help or hurt. Toxicity is the opposite case: no method matches its prompting baseline, indicating that all-layer steering on OLMo-7B is largely ineffective for this concept. On Sycophancy and Unfaithfulness, the best steering configuration clearly exceeds the prompting baseline. Overall, the best steering configuration beats prompting on 3/4 concepts. Across the full 112 -run sweep, however, the steered dataset outperforms the prompting baseline in only 29 runs ( 26% ). These successes concentrate in Unfaithfulness ( 19 ), followed by Sycophancy ( 8) and Hallucination ( 2); Toxicity yields none. The low incidence indicates that downstream utility warrants a careful balance of success, coherence, and diversity. Section 5.4 examines which validation-time signals predict this regime.

## 6 Conclusion 

We evaluated activation steering as a generator of rare HHH-violating data for safety detection, focusing on two areas not investigated in the literature: diversity and downstream utility . Intrinsically, steering success rises with steering strength while coherence and diversity degrade. Extrinsically, detectors fine-tuned on the best steered configurations beat a prompting baseline on 3 of 4 concepts, but downstream utility lives in a narrow regime where success, coherence, and diversity are jointly satisfied. The harmonic mean of these three axes correlates with downstream AUROC consistently enough to serve as a practical tuning target. Practitioners can navigate the vast hyperparameter space of AS more effectively. Overall, our results provide a grounded heuristic for AS hyperparameter tuning and highlight the potential of AS as a synthetic data generator for specific target concepts. 

## Limitations 

Our study has several scope limitations. (i) Hyperparameter scope: AS admits many hyperparameters (Appendix A), and we fixed most of the hyperparameters to practically justifiable values. Given the sensitivity of hyperparameters in AS[ 58 , 25 , 11 , 12 , 48 , 34 , 52 ], changes to fixed values may affect our findings, and the extent of impact is not measured in our experiments. (ii) Small sample size: 

112 fine-tuning runs reduce to only 28 extrinsic measurements per dataset. Hence, the correlation between intrinsic and extrinsic metrics has limited generalizability. However, the consistency of the positive correlation between our heuristic and AUROC across concepts and ablations is therefore a stronger argument than any single coefficient. (iii) Length bias: compression ratio and n-gram diversity are length-sensitive; truncation reduces but does not eliminate this bias. (iv) Effect of model scale: We primarily focused on only one model family (Olmo) with two checkpoints ( 7 and 32 B). Hence, the better steering outcomes for smaller model can only be interpreted for Olmo-7B. 

## Broader Impacts 

The overarching goal of our study is to explore and investigate existing methods to improve safety detection models. However, we acknowledge the potential risks of misuse of our work. To mitigate 9the negative impact of the misuse, we propose to release code but not the steered generations, leaving a compute barrier and audit trail for reproduction. We provide a more detailed discussion in Appendix A.

Intent and scope. The goal of this work is to strengthen safety-detection classifiers that flag harmful outputs from language models, addressing the chronic scarcity of HHH-violating training examples. We do not repurpose activation steering as a tool for producing harmful content; we evaluate whether it can strengthen safety detection. The steering methods we evaluate are established in prior literature [41, 5, 58]; we contribute analysis, not new techniques. 

Positive impact. Safety detectors gate LLM deployment but require harmful examples that are scarce and ethically fraught to source. Our work characterises when activation steering can supply such examples synthetically. The proposed heuristic (harmonic mean of success, coherence, and diversity) helps practitioners discard low-utility configurations during validation, reducing wasted compute and the risk of training detectors on poor data. 

Negative impact. The same machinery can produce harmful content for non-defensive use. (i) Misuse: A motivated actor could apply these methods to open-weight models to generate toxic, sycophantic, hallucinated, or unfaithful text at scale; our narrow-regime finding provides partial information about which configurations are most effective. (ii) False security: Detectors trained on steered data may overfit to the artefacts we document (length drift, diversity collapse) and fail on real-world content, which is concerning if such detectors are treated as hard safety gates. (iii) Asymmetric error costs: High false-positive rates could disproportionately suppress speech from groups whose language patterns differ from the model‚Äôs training distribution; we do not audit this property. 

Mitigations. (1) Gated release: we release code and configurations but not the steered generations, leaving a compute barrier and audit trail for reproduction. (2) Defence in depth: AS-trained detectors should be one component of a layered safety system, not a single gate. (3) Real-world validation: 

detectors must be validated on an independent corpus of real harmful content before deployment, not on held-out synthetic data alone. (4) Red-team asymmetry: defensive use of our heuristic is operationally simpler than offensive use, which additionally requires evading deployed safety systems. Our central finding ‚ÄĒ that downstream utility lies in a narrow regime jointly satisfying success, coherence, and diversity ‚ÄĒ indicates that reliable misuse via steering is more constrained than commonly assumed. 

LLM usage disclosure. All prose in this paper was drafted by the human authors; large language models (LLMs) were used only to edit and tighten already-written text, never to generate drafts of any section. We additionally used LLMs to convert tabular data from CSV into LaTeX format and to assist with visualisation. LLM-based coding assistants supported routine data-processing tasks and the implementation of well-defined statistical procedures such as Pearson correlations and paired 

t-tests.

## D Detailed Downstream Detection Results 

In this section, we provide the AUROC and F1-scores for all our fine-tuning experiments, including ablations. All of our fine-tuning experiments are conducted with a replacement protocol, i.e., steered or prompting generations replace the true positives in the available training data. Our preference for the replacement protocol over augmentation was based on two key reasons. First, replacement controls the dataset size, lowering compute cost. Second, in augmentation, post-fine-tuning AUROC collapses into a narrow band: the original training data combined with a strong Mistral-7B classifier handles noisy positives well, so even low-utility steering reaches the original-data baseline. This collapse erases the signal needed to differentiate bad, moderate, and good steering configurations. Replacement instead spreads configurations from near-random to near-ceiling, preserving the dynamic range needed for our analysis. Table 5: Performance metrics across datasets and steering methods. F.4 Prompt Optimization for the Prompting Baseline 

For the adversarial prompts used for the prompting baseline (Section 4.1); we iteratively optimised them over 

4‚Äď6 rounds before freezing them for the main sweep. For each concept, we started from a first version, generated a small sample of responses, and inspected whether the outputs (i) exhibited the target concept at a high rate and (ii) were coherent and on-topic. We then added components such as a data-generation framing, explicit definitions of the target concept, format constraints (sentence limits, no reasoning), and output-start markers. The final templates in Section F reflect this tuning and should be treated as engineered prompting performance; naive or unoptimised prompts would likely produce weaker baselines and inflate the apparent margin of AS over prompting.
