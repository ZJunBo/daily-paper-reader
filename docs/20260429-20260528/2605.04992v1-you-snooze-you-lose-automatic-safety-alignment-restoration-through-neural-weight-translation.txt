Abstract 

The open-source ecosystem has accelerated the democratization of Large Language Models (LLMs) through the public distribution of specialized Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) modules. However, integrating these third-party adapters often induces catastrophic forgetting of the base model’s foundational safety alignment. Restor-ing these guardrails via fine-tuning on safety data introduces an opposing failure mode: the severe degradation of the specialized domain knowledge the adapter was originally designed to pro-vide. To overcome this zero-resource challenge, we propose Neural Weight Translation (NeWTral), a framework that directly maps unsafe, domain-specific adapters onto a safe alignment manifold while rigorously preserving their core expertise. NeWTral operates as a non-linear translation module pre-trained on a diverse corpus of unsafe-to-safe adapter pairs. By executing this mapping entirely within the parameter space, NeWTral utilizes an adaptive Mixture of Experts (MoE) routing strategy to autonomously blend high-fidelity surgical translators and aggressive alignment experts. We evaluate our framework across four architectural families (Llama, Mistral, Qwen, and Gemma) at scales up to 72B parameters across eight di-verse scientific and professional domains. Our results demonstrate that the MoE variant achieves a radical reduction in the average Attack Success Rate (ASR), dropping from 70% in unsafe experts to just 13%, while maintaining an exceptional 90% average knowledge fidelity. Much like the crowdsourced adapters it remedies, the NeW-Tral module is designed as a standalone, downloadable asset that allows practitioners to restore safety alignment instantly without requiring access to original training data or hardware-intensive retraining. 1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown strong performance in language understanding and generation of responses across a wide range of tasks [ 16 , 26 , 109 , 127 ]. However, despite their general capabilities, LLMs often fall short in specialized areas that require domain knowledge. Studies across domains, including medicine, law, finance, and more, identify several limitations in LLMs, but not limited to hallucinated content, reduced factual accuracy, and errors in multi-step reasoning tasks [56, 72–74, 77]. Fine-tuning is a common approach for adapting LLMs to domain-specific tasks by training on curated datasets, improving task align-ment [ 5, 138 , 143 ]. However, full fine-tuning is computationally expensive and time-consuming [ 49 ]. Parameter-efficient methods such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [ 54 ] address this limitation by freezing pretrained weights and introducing low-rank adapters, thereby reducing the number of trainable parameters. A key chal-lenge in domain-specific fine-tuning is the degradation of previ-ously learned behaviors, particularly safety. Prior work shows that aligned LLMs remain vulnerable to downstream fine-tuning on datasets containing unsafe or noisy examples, often acquiring un-safe behaviors more rapidly [ 108 , 141 , 145 ]. While subsequent safety fine-tuning can partially restore alignment, it frequently reduces performance on domain-specific tasks. We refer to this trade-off between safety and task performance as the utility–alignment tax. The conventional mitigation to this phenomenon is to mix safety-oriented data with domain data during the supervised fine-tuning phase. However, in real-world deployment, especially within the de-centralized open-source AI ecosystem, this mitigation is not always applicable. Frequently, practitioners rely on pre-trained, highly specialized adapters downloaded from third-party repositories. In these zero-resource scenarios, the original domain training data is entirely inaccessible due to proprietary restrictions, data privacy regulations, or storage expiration, rendering traditional data-mixing strategies mathematically impossible. Similarly, an organization can train an adapter on proprietary data but needs to restore safety alignment in the model without access to safety-oriented data. Recent post-hoc interventions attempt to address this zero-data bottleneck by directly manipulating the parameter space using static orthogonal projections (e.g., Safe LoRA [ 53 ], SaLoRA [ 79 ]). Yet, these approaches inherently assume that safety and domain utility occupy linearly separable subspaces. As we demonstrate, this assumption fails in complex professional domains where special-ized reasoning is deeply entangled with potential dual-use harm. Consequently, linear weight interventions indiscriminately over-write expert weights, resulting in a severe utility-alignment tax: the model either remains vulnerable to harmful questions or suffers a catastrophic collapse of its specialized knowledge.

To resolve this fundamental issue, we propose Neural Weight Translation (NeWTral), a post-hoc alignment framework that op-erates directly on a fully-trained adapter without modifying the original training pipeline, restoring foundational safety guardrails directly in the parameter space. Crucially, NeWTral is zero-shot at deployment time: given a previously unseen unsafe adapter, the pre-trained translator requires no gradient-based optimization, no tar-get domain data, and no safe reference adapter. Rather than relying on rigid linear algebraic projections, NeWTral frames safety restora-tion as a non-linear mapping between disjoint parameter subspaces. Crucially, the NeWTral module is designed to be trained a priori and distributed as a standalone asset. Much like the crowdsourced adapters it remedies, practitioners simply download the pre-trained NeWTral translator and apply it directly to their proprietary or third-party adapters. Distributed as a pre-trained meta-model, it allows practitioners to secure unverified or proprietary adapters zero-shot at deployment time. We term this process zero-shot as it requires no gradient-based optimization on the target adapter and no access to the target’s underlying training data. As demonstrated by our experimental campaign, having a mono-lithic translation strategy makes it difficult to achieve an optimal balance between utility and safety. To address this, NeWTral em-ploys an autonomous, weight-space Mixture of Experts (MoE) rout-ing strategy. By analyzing the statistical signatures of the unsafe adapter’s weights, layer by layer, the MoE router dynamically blends the outputs of two specialized translation networks: a Surgical Ex-pert optimized to preserve depth and raw domain knowledge, and an Aggressive Expert designed to inject robust refusal heuristics and ethical compliance precisely where dual-use risks manifest. We rigorously evaluate NeWTral across a massive experimen-tal campaign encompassing four architectural families (Llama-3.1, Mistral, Qwen-2.5, and Gemma-2) scaling up to 72B parameters, tested on eight diverse, high-stakes domains (including medicine, law, finance, and physics). Our framework drastically outperforms existing state-of-the-art post-hoc alignment strategies. 

• We introduce NeWTral, the first non-linear parameter trans-lation framework for restoring safety in domain-specific LoRA adapters. Distributed as a pre-trained asset, it allows practitioners to secure unverified or proprietary adapters zero-shot at deployment time, without requiring target training data or any hardware-intensive retraining. 

• We propose a layer-aware Mixture of Experts architecture that autonomously balances surgical utility preservation and aggressive ethical policing, successfully decoupling safety alignment from the utility-alignment tax. 

• We conduct a deep internal representation analysis, pro-viding empirical evidence that safety alignment is highly heterogeneous across network depth. We demonstrate that MLP blocks are the primary drivers of behavioral safety, whereas attention blocks must remain untouched to pre-serve domain reasoning. 

• Through an evaluation across model scales up to 72B param-eters and 8 domains, we show that the MoE variant reduces the global average Attack Success Rate (ASR), dropping from ∼70% in unsafe experts to ∼13%, while maintaining an exceptional ∼90% knowledge fidelity.

3.2 Neural Weight Translation 

NeWTral is a post-hoc framework for mitigating safety degradation in domain-specialized adapters. It operates at deployment time on unseen adapters without requiring retraining or access to domain or safety data. As discussed in Section 3.1, integrating LoRA adapters often weakens safety alignment in favor of domain-specific behav-ior. In practice, such adapters are typically sourced externally, with training data unavailable due to privacy or proprietary constraints. This renders standard fine-tuning-based alignment impractical and often incurs a utility–alignment tax, degrading domain perfor-mance. We address this setting by modeling alignment restoration as a learned mapping between adapter parameter configurations. NeWTral learns a transformation from unsafe adapter parameters to safety-aligned parameters using a neural network operating directly in parameter space. This enables targeted parameter modifications that reduce unsafe outputs while preserving domain-specific behav-ior. The resulting model can be applied post hoc to existing adapters without access to their original training data, enabling alignment adjustments without additional task-specific fine-tuning. Let 𝑀 

denote a base model aligned with generic knowledge. We specialize this model to a specific domain using LoRA. In particular, we con-sider two distinct versions of the same adapter: one adapter 𝑊 𝑑𝑜𝑚 ,trained exclusively on domain-specific data D𝑑𝑜𝑚 , as introduced in Section 3.1, to obtain a domain-expert adapter; and one adapter 

𝑊 𝑠𝑎𝑓 𝑒 , trained on a combined dataset that includes both domain-specific data and safety-oriented data, D𝑆𝐷 = D𝑑𝑜𝑚 ∪ D 𝑠𝑎𝑓 𝑒 . The objective of NeWTral is produce a mapping model T such that: 

T ( 𝑊 𝑑𝑜𝑚 ) = 𝑊 𝑐𝑢𝑟𝑒𝑑 ≈ 𝑊 𝑠𝑎𝑓 𝑒 . (8) Crucially, this transformation is performed zero-shot from the practitioner’s perspective. While T is optimized a priori on a di-verse corpus of adapter pairs, its application to a specific, unverified domain adapter at deployment time is a pure inference operation. Because the translator has learned the underlying geometric re-lationship between the unsafe and safe subspace, it can project a previously unseen weight vector 𝑊 𝑑𝑜𝑚 onto its safe counterpart without requiring any additional “shots” (i.e., training samples) of the domain-specific data or further fine-tuning of the model parameters. Prior work on linear parameter-space interventions suggests that safety and domain knowledge are not cleanly separa-ble, often leading to significant utility degradation. Our empirical results (Section 4.1.5) further support this observation, showing that linear transformations fail to preserve domain expertise in complex settings. This motivates modeling alignment restoration as a non-linear mapping, which can better capture the interaction between domain-specific representations and safety constraints. Such transformations enable targeted behavioral adjustments while preserving the high-frequency structure of expert knowledge.

Adaptive Alignment via Mixture of Experts . While a Static NeWTral translator effectively maps safe to unsafe, it inherently optimizes for fixed regions of the safety-utility tax. As we will demonstrate in our experiments, the optimal alignment strategy is domain-dependent. Scientific domains typically require a surgi-cal translation to preserve knowledge depth, whereas institutional domains often require an aggressive translation to enforce ethical compliance and initiate a structural persona shift. Consequently, relying on a single, monolithic translator imposes an inflexible trade-off across heterogeneous domains. To solve this, we propose a translator that mixes the knowledge of multiple experts, obtain-ing a Mixture of Experts (MoE) NeWTral. The MoE dynamically blends the outputs of two pre-trained, frozen translation experts, each possessing distinct behavioral characteristics. The first expert acts as Surgical Expert T𝑠𝑢𝑟𝑔 optimized on high fidelity, ensuring minimal semantic drift from the original domain expert. The sec-ond is characterized by a more aggressive NeWTral obtaining an Aggressive Expert T𝑎𝑔𝑔𝑟 optimized for maximum safety alignment, effectively improving robust safety heuristics and ethical policing. 4 Experiments 4.1 Experimental Setup 

4.1.1 Dataset .

All the datasets are from HuggingFace and GitHub. 

Adapters dataset - To obtain the adapters employed in the training and evaluation of the NewTral modules, we instantiated 10 distinct adapters for each of the eight selected domains, with each adapter trained on a different dataset specific to that do-main. For safety alignment, we utilized the standard PKU-SafeRLHF dataset [68]. 

Evaluation Prompts - To measure the safety-utility trade-off, we employ a composite benchmark of 1,760 prompts per model con-figuration. This corpus integrates: (i) benign expert queries to evalu-ate knowledge retention and epistemic fidelity; (ii) domain-specific harmful prompts designed to exploit specialized technical exper-tise; and (iii) general adversarial queries from JBB-Behaviors [ 22 ]to assess foundational safety resilience. This multifaceted setup ensures rigorous accounting for both specialized utility and robust alignment (see Appendix B.1). 

4.1.2 LLMs . To validate our approach, we evaluated LLMs from four architectural families with varying parameter scales. They are Llama-3.1- 8B & 70B [ 46 ], Mistral-7B-v0.3 [ 69 ], Qwen2.5- 7B & 72B [124, 137], and Gemma-2- 9B & 27B [122, 123]. 

4.1.3 NeWTral models . The evaluated architectures include Au-toEncoder (AE), Conditional Variational AutoEncoder (CVAE), Flow Matching (FM), and Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), which are depth-static, as well as a Mixture of Experts model as a depth-dynamic translator. Additional details on architectures and training are in Appendix B.6. 

4.1.4 System Configuration . Specification of the system used to run experiments in Appendix B.2 

4.1.5 Compared Baselines . In our experimental study, we com-pare our method with two state-of-the-art baselines: Safe LoRA [ 53 ]and SaLoRA [ 79 ]. It is important to note how these baselines in-tersect with our deployment scenario. Safe LoRA is not adapted; we evaluate it using its original methodology, which requires ac-cess to the safe target-domain adapter. Although this bypasses the zero-resource limitation of our threat model, it provides a strong benchmark for what static linear projections can achieve when fully resourced. On the other hand, SaLoRA is natively a training-time intervention that we adapt into a post-hoc linear filter, placing it at a disadvantage. By including both, we aim to demonstrate that our learned non-linear translation (NeWTral) outperforms linear parameter-space interventions, whether they are constrained by our post-hoc scenario or provided with ideal reference data. Additional details are in Appendix B.5. 

4.1.6 Settings . The settings used to perform the following exper-imental campaign are reported in Appendix B.4. 

4.1.7 Evaluation & Metrics . We evaluate our framework using two primary metrics: Attack Success Rate (ASR) and Knowledge Re-tention (KR) . ASR is determined via an ensemble of three evaluators: Llama-Guard-3-8B [ 61 , 83 ], the SecureBreak safety classifier [ 6], and a keyword-based refusal signal. KR measures the semantic fidelity of cured model responses against the unsafe domain ex-pert on benign queries, capturing preservation of the fine-tuning delta rather than base model behavior, using cosine similarity (via Sentence-Transformers [ 112 ]) and BERTScore [ 144 ]. Residual un-safe content on benign prompts is independently captured by ASR. The Final Score is the harmonic mean of (1 − ASR ) and KR , pro-viding a single safety-utility trade-off metric. Full implementation details are in Appendix B.3.

5 Limitations and Future Work 

While NeWTral’s MoE variant successfully finds the safety-utility trade-off through autonomous weight-space routing, several open challenges remain. The method does not fully eliminate unsafe outputs, particularly where harmful and benign content are closely intertwined. The eight training domains comprise ten datasets from heterogeneous sources, exposing the translator to substantial intra-domain distributional variation, distinct sub-topics, difficulty lev-els, and linguistic registers, thereby encouraging the extraction of domain-agnostic safety geometry rather than domain-specific surface patterns. Generalization to structurally novel domains re-mains an open direction. Generating paired (unsafe, safe) adapters requires access to safety data during NeWTral’s pre-training phase, even if not at deployment time. Practitioners who cannot produce such pairs must rely on a community-distributed NeWTral mod-ule, analogous to the community-distributed domain adapters this framework remediates. The routing network learns a fixed interpo-lation policy. Future iterations could incorporate a steerable con-ditioning parameter, allowing stakeholders to calibrate risk toler-ance continuously, from expert depth (low 𝑔 ) to maximum ethical policing (high 𝑔 ), without retraining. The current binary mixture demonstrates the principle of weight-space gating but does not address orthogonal harm categories independently. Future work should explore specialized experts targeting Personally Identifiable Information leakage, algorithmic bias, or software vulnerabilities, enabling granular structural interventions without persona drift. 

6 Related Work 

The vulnerability of aligned LLMs to catastrophic safety forget-ting during fine-tuning is well-documented [ 41 , 70 , 142 ]. While pre-trained models are aligned using techniques such as RLHF to embed safety guardrails, subsequent fine-tuning on domain-specific datasets that lack safety-focused examples often degrades these safety alignments [ 29 , 67 ]. This issue occurs due to Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques, especially LoRA, which can erode the latent safety structures formed during pre-training [ 78 ,82 ]. As a result, models that are fine-tuned for narrow applications (such as medical diagnosis or financial analysis) become signifi-cantly more vulnerable to domain-specific jailbreaks, harmful obe-dience to malicious prompts, and privacy breaches. It has been shown that LoRA adapters can be leveraged to conduct auditing or mitigate flaws in base models [ 8]. Recent work tries to separate task performance from safety by directly modifying model parameters. With this goal, Safe LoRA [ 53 ] demonstrated that safety alignment is contained within controllable parameter subspaces. More pre-cisely, its core approach involves computing the difference between safe and unsafe versions of the same adapter, then applying a linear projection to adjust the corrupted weights into a safe configura-tion during post-training. Similarly, SaLoRA [ 79 ] addresses this issue during the training phase of the unsafe adapters. In this case, the projection of the safe weights is performed by introducing an auxiliary safety module that encourages the weights to grow or-thogonally to the pre-existing safety weights, thereby minimizing interference. Despite safety alignment, LLMs are still vulnerable to jailbreak attacks. Recent research exploits these vulnerabilities, revealing that safety mechanisms often fail under targeted structural analysis. For instance, the XBreaking [ 7 ] approach leverages AI explain-ability to compare censored and uncensored models to identify exploitable alignment patterns, demonstrating how targeted noise injection can effectively shatter alignment constraints. More on this, the NeuroStrike attack [ 135 ] is a framework demonstrating that modern alignment heavily relies on sparse, specialized safety neu-rons. By pruning a small subset of neurons, NeuroStrike achieves a high ASR without relying on traditional trial-and-error prompt optimization. In addition, GateBreaker [ 134 ], a training-free attack that weakens MoE LLM safety by identifying and disabling a small set of safety-critical neurons. It shows that safety is highly local-ized and vulnerable, enabling large increases in ASR with minimal performance loss and generalizes across models and modalities. Recent literature emphasizes evaluating defense-enhanced LLMs systematically, accounting for the diverse array of attack vectors and configurations, often referred to as a "bag of tricks" [ 136 ]. To this end, JailbreakBench [ 22 ] was introduced as an open robust-ness benchmark to unify the evaluation of jailbreak attacks and defenses under a reproducible framework. Although explicitly de-fending against jailbreaks is not the primary focus of our work, our proposed structural modifications intrinsically impact the model’s weight representations. Therefore, to rigorously assess the safety implications and adversarial resilience of our approach, we evaluate our cured models using the standardized JailbreakBench framework, demonstrating that our method preserves (and potentially fortifies) the model’s safety alignment under adversarial conditions. While our work builds upon the weight-space discoveries of Safe LoRA and SaLoRA, it departs from them in two fundamen-tal ways. First, rather than relying on static linear projections or altered training loops, NeWTral models the safety subspace as a non-linear mapping. Second, instead of extracting safety vectors from an isolated domain, our translators are trained concurrently across diverse, high-stakes fields. This multi-domain exposure prevents overfitting, enabling the network to learn a generalized geometric representation of safety that transfers reliably across heterogeneous professional datasets. 

7 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced NeWTral, a zero-shot at deployment time projection framework that mitigates the catastrophic safety degradation induced by specialized LoRA adapters. By exploiting the structural heterogeneity of Transformer architectures, we de-veloped an autonomous weight-space MoE router that balances safety re-alignment with the preservation of expert knowledge. Our evaluations across eight technical domains and scales up to 72B parameters demonstrate that NeWTral achieves a superior safety-utility trade-off compared to existing linear baselines. These results prove that complex behavioral constraints can be systematically encoded and transferred strictly within the parameter space, bypass-ing the need for hardware-intensive gradient optimization. Future work will extend this framework to include steerable conditioning parameters and explore its efficacy in mitigating orthogonal secu-rity threats, such as targeted backdoors and continuous distribution shifts. B Additional Details about the Experimental Setup B.1 Datasets 

This section reports additional details of the dataset used in training and testing of the adapters. 

Training - The domain-specific (knowledge) data consist of eight distinct and diverse domains: medical, mathematics, finan-cial, legal, academic, physics, biomedical, and chemistry. For each domain, 10 unique datasets, which are available in the form of question-answer (Q&A) pairs, are used for the generation of the domain adapters. For safety, we use the standard dataset PKU-SafeRLHF [ 68 ], released by the PKU-Alignment Team at Peking University. It is a large-scale human preference dataset specifically developed to support safety alignment research in LLMs. This, to-gether with the domain adapters, is used for the generation of safe adapters. 

Testing - Our knowledge dataset comprises 160 Q&A pairs sourced from HuggingFace Datasets, distributed evenly across do-mains as in training. Each domain includes 20 carefully chosen Q&A pairs covering a wide range of topics and difficulty levels within the field to assess the knowledge of the cured adapters. To assess the safety of the adapters, we merged two types of datasets: 50 harmful knowledge questions from each domain and a set of harm-ful prompts from the JBB-Behaviors dataset [ 22 ]. JBB-Behaviors is used as a stress test, as it spans a wide range of harmful behaviors across ten categories aligned with OpenAI usage policies, including disinformation, fraud, harassment, and physical harm. This com-bined setup allows us to evaluate both domain-specific risks and general safety awareness. • AutoEncoder Model: 

The autoencoder translator takes the most direct approach among all models: the unsafe weight vector is compressed into a low-dimensional latent representation and then decoded into the safe weight vector. The bottleneck at 𝐿 = 256 forces the network to discard unsafe-specific detail and retain only the information nec-essary to reconstruct a safe output, acting as an implicit regulariser under the small dataset regime. LayerNorm appears only at the encoder’s first projection, nor-malizing the heterogeneous weight magnitudes before compression. The decoder is purely affine with ReLU activations and no normal-ization, allowing it to freely reconstruct the original weight scale. 

Training Details - The model is trained to minimize the MSE between the decoded output and the ground-truth safe weights The optimiser is Adam with learning rate 1 × 10 −3, trained for 150 epochs with batch size 1. • Conditional Variational AutoEncoder (CVAE) Model: 

The Conditional VAE (CVAE) is chosen over direct regression be-cause the unsafe-to-safe mapping is treated as a conditional gen-erative problem rather than a deterministic function. The encoder sees both Wdom and Wsafe at training time, learning what transfor-mation residual must be stored in the latent variable z. At inference only the unsafe weights are available, so z is drawn from the prior 

N ( 0, I) and decoded with Wdom as conditioning. The KL term regu-larises the posterior toward this prior, ensuring that prior samples remain meaningful even with very few training pairs. KL annealing (ramping 𝛽 from 0 to 1 over the first 40% of training) prevents poste-rior collapse, allowing the decoder to learn a useful reconstruction before the KL penalty becomes active. E Performance on Harmful JBB-Behaviors (JailbreakBench) 

In line with previous evaluations carried out and discussed in Sec-tion 4.2, we assess our approach across several scenarios and subse-quently focus on a more structured evaluation. To align with general safety objectives, we use harmful prompts from the JBB-Behaviors dataset to evaluate model behavior under potentially unsafe con-ditions. The dataset was selected because it contains 100 unique misuse behaviors grouped into ten major categories aligned with OpenAI’s usage policies, namely: Disinformation, Economic harm, Expert advice, Fraud/Deception, Government decision-making, Ha-rassment/Discrimination, Malware/Hacking, Physical harm, Pri-vacy, and Sexual/Adult content. Together with this domain-diverse dataset, the setup enables a systematic analysis of the robustness and generalization capabilities of the generated adapters Table 10 presents detailed ASR results for the selected models evaluated on harmful inputs. As expected, the original Unsafe do-main experts show the highest safety failure on the JBB dataset; the results show a clear degradation in safety with higher ASR across all methods and the three safety evaluation metrics and architectures. Across the different scores, the curing methods show a clear and consistent hierarchy in effectiveness when ordered from weaker to stronger performance in reducing ASR. Starting with Safe LoRA and SaLoRA, these methods provide only moderate improvements over the unsafe baselines and are generally the least effective among all approaches. Safe LoRA oc-casionally reduces ASR in a meaningful way, but its performance is inconsistent across models. SaLoRA, in particular, often fails to improve robustness and, in some cases, performs worse than Safe LoRA, suggesting that its modification of the LoRA adaptation does not reliably align with safety objectives when JBB harmful ques-tions are considered. Consistently, our NeWTral framework with different translator variations, such as AE, CVAE, FM, MLP, and MoE, outperforms the Safe LoRA and SaLoRA across all the models. AE and CVAE both provide intermediate performance by learn-ing latent representations that aim to suppress unsafe behaviors. CVAE tends to be more stable and slightly more effective than AE, which achieves lower ASR. Although both methods consistently outperform LoRA-based approaches, their overall reductions in ASR remain moderate, indicating that reconstruction-based objectives alone are insufficient for strong adversarial robustness. 0 5 10 15 20 25 30

By enforcing structured transformations in representation space, FM better aligns model outputs with safety constraints. However, despite its stability, it does not consistently achieve the lowest ASR values, leaving room for more expressive methods to outperform it. The MLP-based approach marks a significant improvement, often achieving very low ASR, particularly on larger models. This sug-gests that learned projection layers can effectively filter unsafe di-rections in the representation space. Nevertheless, its performance is not uniformly consistent across all architectures, indicating some sensitivity to model scale and distributional variation. Finally, the MoE method achieves the best overall performance, demonstrating a superior Pareto-optimal balance between safety and utility. While the standalone Aggressive (MLP) expert occa-sionally yields lower absolute ASR values in specific configurations, such as Llama-3.1-70B and Qwen2.5-72B, it does so by incurring a prohibitive utility-alignment tax. By contrast, the MoE’s routing-based design enables selective processing that prioritizes the Sur-gical Expert for knowledge-dense layers while escalating safety injection only where adversarial risk is detected. Consequently, MoE consistently yields the highest aggregate Final Score across all architectures, establishing itself as the most robust and scal-able strategy for restoring safety alignment without sacrificing the model’s specialized mechanistic depth. Figure 5 presents a comparative evaluation of multiple alignment strategies, i.e., unsafe baseline(finetuned), Safe LoRA, SaLoRA, and our MoE approach across a diverse set of risk-sensitive dimensions for several language models. The figure represent a average ASR of all the three defined ASR across different domains of the JBB dataset. Across nearly all models and categories, the Unsafe Finetuned condition forms the outer envelope, indicating the highest propen-sity for unsafe or unregulated outputs. In contrast, NeWTral (MoE) consistently yields the most compact profiles, demonstrating strong suppression of harmful behaviors across all evaluated categories. SafeLoRA achieves a balanced reduction in risk, while SaLoRA ex-hibits comparatively higher variance, occasionally approaching the unsafe baseline in sensitive domains such as fraud/deception and expert advice. A consistent trend emerges across model scales; larger models generally exhibit improved controllability, with tighter distribu-tions under aligned methods and clearer separation from the unsafe baseline. However, certain categories—most notably fraud/decep-tion and expert advice—remain challenging. These findings suggest that while alignment techniques significantly mitigate risk, their ef-fectiveness is domain-dependent and benefits from increased model capacity. The results highlight the importance of both alignment strategy and model scale in achieving robust safety performance. NeWTral (MoE) demonstrates superior and consistent risk minimization, sug-gesting that a mixture-of-experts-based alignment offers a promis-ing direction for fine-grained control.
