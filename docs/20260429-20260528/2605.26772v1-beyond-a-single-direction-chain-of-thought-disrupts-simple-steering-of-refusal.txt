# Abstract 

Large reasoning models (LRMs) generate chain-of-thought (CoT) traces before producing final outputs, introducing a dynamic internal state that may complicate control mechanisms such as re-fusal. Unlike instruction-tuned LLMs, where re-fusal is mediated by a single directional subspace, refusal in large reasoning models (LRMs) addi-tionally depends on the CoT. In DeepSeek-R1-Distill-LLaMA-8B, activation steering reverses refusal in only 39% of cases when the CoT is kept fixed, but removing the CoT entirely increases this to 70%, indicating that the CoT actively rein-forces refusal. In a two-stage intervention where the model regenerates its CoT under activation steering, refusal is reversed in 94% of cases, while the resulting CoT alone retains 48% of this effect even after steering is removed. This suggests that the CoT can carry and reconstruct the compliance signal independently. These findings indicate that refusal in LRMs is jointly encoded in residual stream activations and CoT. This joint activation makes LRM more robust against activation-level interventions alone, but exposes CoT to a possible alternative surface attack.

In LLMs, refusal has been characterized as a low-dimensional mechanism mediated by a single direction in the residual stream (Arditi et al., 2024). Concretely, this direction is localized at the end-of-instruction (EOI) token and steering along it reliably shifts model behavior between compliance and refusal (Arditi et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2025b). # 2. Related Work 

Refusal Mechanism in LLM. Recent studies characterize refusal as a low-rank phenomenon mediated by a single ”refusal direction” in the residual stream (Arditi et al., 2024). This mechanism operates independently of the model’s abil-ity to recognize harm, as refusal and harmfulness are en-coded separately in the activation space (Zhao et al., 2025b). Furthermore, these safety circuits are often anchored within template tokens rather than the instruction semantics (Leong et al., 2025). These works provide a detailed characteri-zation of refusal in LLMs. However, it remains unclear whether the same mechanisms hold in LRMs, where the model generates an intermediate CoT before producing a final response. We investigate this by examining how activation-level refusal signals interact with the presence and generation of the CoT in LRMs. 

Adversarial Attacks in LRM. The explicit reasoning trace introduces novel attack surfaces. Fabricated reasoning paths can trick models into treating safety checks as already passed (Giskard AI, 2024), and prepending long benign reasoning dilutes refusal signals before harmful instructions (Zhao et al., 2025a). Beyond prompt-level attacks, steer-ing or ablating a caution direction specifically during CoT generation significantly increases compliance (Yamaguchi et al., 2025). These results show that manipulating the CoT, whether through prompt-level forgery or activation-level steering, is sufficient to override refusal. However, they treat the CoT primarily as a passive target for intervention, without examining how it interacts with activation level signals or to what extent it can independently maintain or reconstruct refusal. We address this by systematically isolat-ing the individual and joint contributions of residual stream activations and CoT generation to the refusal signal. 

# 3. Experimental Framework/ Technical Preliminaries 

3.1. Dataset and Model 

The training set consists of 100 harmful instructions, sam-pled from ADVBENCH (Zou et al., 2023), MALICIOUSIN-STRUCT (Huang et al., 2024), TDC2023 (Mazeika et al., 2023), and HARMBENCH (Mazeika et al., 2024), which are all refused by DeepSeek-R1-Distill-LLaMA-8B model, along with 100 harmless instructions sampled from Al-paca (Taori et al., 2023). These two subsets are used to derive the refusal and compliance directions. We measure refusal of harmful instructions using the safety score met-ric, introduced by (Arditi et al., 2024). We found that the commonly used phrase-based heuristics for refusal (e.g., “I am sorry”) are less reliable for LRMs, as discussed in more detail in Appendix Section A. For evaluation, we construct a held-out test set compris-ing 100 harmful instructions drawn from JAILBREAK-BENCH (Chao et al., 2024). These samples are also all refused by the DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B model. All experiments are conducted using the DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B model with greedy decoding to ensure repro-ducibility.

# 4. Experiments and Results 

To understand refusal in LRMs, we investigate the contribu-tions of residual stream activations and (CoT). First, the CoT inhibits the effectiveness of activation steer-ing. By comparing steering with a fixed CoT, steering with-out a CoT, and steering with CoT regeneration, we show that the CoT partially resists internal activation shifts unless the CoT is permitted to align with the steering signal. This suggests refusal is not mediated by a single refusal direction alone. Second, LRMs refuse via parallel signals from both the CoT and the residual stream. We demonstrate this re-dundancy by applying a previously steered, compliant CoT without active steering, which quantifies the CoT’s indepen-dent influence. Our results (Table 1) indicate that refusal is mediated jointly by the residual stream activation and the CoT. We evaluate these configurations using the refusal score (Arditi et al., 2024) on 100 harmful, refused instruc-tions, establishing a 0% compliance baseline. 

4.1. Template-Token Steering with Fixed Reasoning Trace 

Steering at template tokens is often sufficient to bypass re-fusal in LLMs (Arditi et al., 2024; Leong et al., 2025), mak-ing it a natural baseline intervention. To test this in LRMs, we steer using the residual stream activations at EOI and EOT final position while holding the CoT fixed. Steering reverses refusal by up to 39% (EOI) and 43% (EOT), with results varying across layers (Figure 1). This effect is signif-icantly weaker than in standard LLMs (Arditi et al., 2024), suggesting a single direction at template tokens cannot fully capture refusal or bypass the factors limiting activation-level control.

4.3. Template-Token Steering with Regenerated Reasoning Trace 

In LLMs, steering allows the model to freely generate all subsequent tokens. In LRMs, the CoT is itself a generated component, and as Section 4.2 suggests, its content actively shapes the final decision. We therefore apply the same EOI-based steering vector while allowing the model to freely regenerate its CoT, consistent with findings that internal interventions during CoT generation can affect model be-havior (Yamaguchi et al., 2025). Since the EOT token is only produced after the CoT, we use the EOI-based steer-ing vector exclusively. This yields a substantial increase in compliance, reaching 94% (Figure 3), compared to 39% when the CoT was fixed (Section 4.1) and 70% when it was suppressed (Section 4.2), matching the performance typically observed in LLMs under steering. Our results sug-gest that steering does not directly flip the final decision but instead biases the CoT generation process, which in turn drives compliant outputs. As shown in Section 4.1, steering alone is insufficient. However, it remains unclear whether the high compliance is driven solely by the compliant CoT or by the combination of steering and a compliant CoT. 5 10 15 20 25 30

4.4. Persistence of CoT Signals. Persistence of CoT Signals. To isolate whether the high compliance in Section 4.3 is driven by the compliant CoT alone or requires both steering and a compliant CoT, we remove steering at inference time and replace the original CoT with one generated under steering. In the absence of steering, compliance drops to 48%, compared to 94% when both steering and CoT regeneration are applied, indicating that the CoT encodes a partial compliance signal that per-sists independently of the steering intervention. However, the substantial drop confirms that the CoT alone cannot fully suppress the refusal signal, as part of it remains encoded in the activations and can only be reduced by additional steering intervention. These results show that the CoT is an active component of the refusal mechanism, capable of ei-ther reinforcing refusal or amplifying compliance depending on its content. 

# 5. Discussion 

Our results reveal that the refusal signal in LRMs is not solely encoded at template tokens, but is jointly maintained by residual stream activations and the CoT. Steering alone yields only 39–43% compliance. Yet, combining steering with a compliant CoT reaches 94%, highlighting the CoT as an active contributor to the refusal mechanism. Two find-ings reinforce the joint encoding of the refusal signal. First, suppressing the CoT raises compliance to 70%, showing that the original reasoning trace actively reconstructs the refusal signal. Secondly, reusing a compliant CoT without steering retains 48% compliance, showing that the trace independently carries a partial compliance signal. Unlike LLMs, where refusal is mediated by a single directional subspace (Arditi et al., 2024), refusal in LRMs is distributed across activations and CoT. This distribution makes LRM more resistant to activation-level interventions, but intro-duces the CoT as an additional attack surface. Our results suggest that detecting a refusal signal in the activations and suppressing CoT could serve as an effective defense mecha-nism, preventing the CoT from being exploited to override or reconstruct the compliance signal. 

# 6. Limitations 

Qualitative Analysis and Faithfulness of Chain-of-Thought. A qualitative inspection reveals systematic dif-ferences in reasoning, such as unsteered CoT emphasizes ethical justifications while steered traces omit them. We did not verify if these patterns hold across all samples. Crucially, our analysis does not establish whether these generated CoT are ”faithful” or causally responsible for the final model be-havior. Although we observe a correlation between ethical reasoning and refusal, we have not tested whether removing these specific reasoning steps would increase compliance. Determining the causal impact of these patterns remains an important direction for future work. 

Single-model Evaluation. All experiments are conducted on a single model (DeepSeek-R1-LLaMA-8B). While this allows for a controlled analysis, it remains unclear whether the observed effects generalize to other chain-of-thought models. Evaluating across a broader set of models is nec-essary to assess the robustness of our findings. # A. Refusal Classification 

To ensure reliable evaluation of refusal behavior, we compare two approaches for automatically classifying model responses as either refused or complied. We construct a dataset of 50 harmful instructions and manually annotate each corresponding response with a binary label (refusal vs. compliance). Responses are classified as refusals if they contain predefined phrases (e.g., “I’m sorry”, “I cannot help with that”,...). • Model-based classifier (Llama-3.2-8B-Guard). A dedicated safety model is used to predict refusal behavior.
