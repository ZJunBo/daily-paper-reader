## Abstract 

Language models are widely used in assistant settings, where controlling behavioral attributes is often essential. Activation steering modifies hidden-state represen-tations at inference time, providing a lightweight, training-free mechanism that can be toggled at runtime. Existing methods, however, have focused primarily on steering a single attribute at a time. When multiple attributes must be controlled si-multaneously, naive summation of per-attribute steering vectors suffers from norm imbalance and directional cancellation, while classifier-based approaches require retraining whenever the attribute set changes. We introduce ORBIT (Orthogonal Rotation-Based Intervention Technique), a training-free extension of rotation-based steering to the multi-attribute setting. Our method constructs a joint subspace from per-attribute steering planes via singular value decomposition and applies a single norm-preserving rotation within that subspace toward a combined target direc-tion. Adaptive per-token gating identifies which attributes need correction at each position, and an optional additive boost strengthens attributes with weak initial projection. We also introduce T RAIT FACTORY , a new multi-attribute benchmark that focuses on behavioral tendencies rather than surface-level style. We evaluate ORBIT on T RAIT FACTORY and T ONE BANK across three models (Llama-3.2-3B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B) while steering multiple attributes simultaneously, showing that it achieves stronger and more balanced multi-attribute steering than existing training-free baselines while better preserving output coherence. 

## 1 Introduction 

Inference-time activation steering modifies hidden-state representations during a forward pass to shift model behavior along a target attribute, without updating any parameters [Turner et al., 2023, Panickssery et al., 2023, Zou et al., 2023, Vu and Nguyen, 2025]. For a single attribute (e.g., making responses more concise, or more cautious), the problem is well studied and effective methods exist. However, many practical scenarios require controlling multiple attributes simultaneously: a response that is both cautious and concise, or an argument that combines empirical grounding with moral framing. Achieving such multi-attribute control at inference time, without retraining, remains a challenging problem. The difficulty is not simply that there are more attributes to handle. Moving from one attribute to several introduces a qualitatively different challenge: geometric interference between attribute directions in activation space. Different attributes may induce steering directions that partially cancel, redundantly overlap, or differ by orders of magnitude in norm. As the number of simultaneous attributes grows, the probability that at least two directions have substantial negative cosine similarity increases, and any composition scheme that ignores this geometry becomes increasingly fragile.

Different attributes produce vectors with vastly different norms; without normalization, high-magnitude attributes dominate. With normalization, absolute scale is controlled, but choosing the αk values is still difficult: we do not know a priori how strongly each attribute should be pushed, and each coefficient interacts with the others. As K grows, tuning these coupled strengths becomes a high-dimensional search that scales poorly. Training-based alternatives, such as gradient-guided classifiers [Oozeer et al., 2025], learned gating functions [Nguyen et al., 2025], and composition operators [Radevski et al., 2026], can sidestep linear summation, but they tie the model to a fixed attribute set: swapping or adding even a single attribute requires new labeled data and retraining, limiting flexible, on-the-fly behavioral composition. Angular Steering [Vu and Nguyen, 2025] formulates single-attribute steering as a norm-preserving rotation in a 2D subspace, subsuming CAA and directional ablation [Arditi et al., 2024] as special cases. However, it is defined for a single feature direction, and its effect can be limited when the target attribute signal is initially weak but stronger induction is needed. Naively composing K

independent 2D rotations in potentially overlapping planes reintroduces the same cancellation and dominance problems, compounded by norm distortion from repeated projections (empirical evidence in Appendix H). 

Our approach. The central challenge in multi-attribute steering is that attribute directions are obtained separately, so applying independent rotations makes them interfere with one another. ORBIT (Orthogonal Rotation-Based Intervention Technique) addresses this by first constructing one shared subspace that captures all target attributes, then applying a single norm-preserving rotation inside that shared subspace. Components outside this subspace are left unchanged. Adaptive per-token gating then applies correction only where needed, with stronger updates for more misaligned tokens. Our contributions are: 1. We introduce ORBIT, a training-free geometric method that constructs a joint trait subspace and applies a single norm-preserving rotation within it; components outside that subspace are left unchanged by construction (§4.2). 2. We study naive multi-attribute composition strategies and characterize their common geo-metric failure modes, then validate them empirically (§3.4, Appendix H). 3. We introduce T RAIT FACTORY , a multi-attribute benchmark of 13 personality traits across 22 topic categories, complementing T ONE BANK (5 conversational tones) (§5). 4. To capture balanced multi-attribute control, we introduce geometric gain (positive only when all attributes improve) and joint success rate (the fraction of targeted attributes that improve), with minimum per-attribute improvement as a complementary metric. 5. We evaluate ORBIT on both benchmarks across three model families (Llama-3.2-3B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B), demonstrating it consistently outperforms baselines, while preserv-ing output coherence. ## 2 Related work 

Activation steering for single attributes. Contrastive activation methods such as ActAdd [Turner et al., 2023], CAA [Panickssery et al., 2023], and RepE [Zou et al., 2023], extract a steering direction from paired prompts and add it to hidden states at inference time. Angular Steering [Vu and Nguyen, 2025] reformulates this as a norm-preserving rotation in a 2D subspace, preserving activation norm by construction. These techniques are effective for a single attribute but do not address how to compose multiple attributes simultaneously. 

Per-sample adaptive steering. Rather than applying a fixed global vector, PIXEL [Yu et al., 2025] computes a sample-specific steering direction and scale at inference time, tailoring intervention intensity to each input. This addresses a complementary challenge, input-dependent dosing, whereas our work targets the composition of multiple attributes without directional interference; the two mechanisms are compatible in principle. 2Multi-attribute composition. Steering multiple attributes at once remains relatively underexplored: van der Weij et al. [2024] found that naive vector addition is “largely unsuccessful” as the number of attributes grows. Existing remedies include heuristic vector summation [CONFST; Song et al., 2025], gradient-guided classifiers [K-Steering; Oozeer et al., 2025], and learned composition operators [MAT-STEER; Nguyen et al., 2025], [MSRS; Jiang et al., 2025], [CST; Radevski et al., 2026]. Learned methods must be retrained whenever the attribute set changes, limiting flexibility. ORBIT addresses multi-attribute composition as a closed-form geometric problem: it constructs a joint subspace via SVD and steers through norm-preserving rotation, requiring no training and generalizing to new attribute combinations without retraining. We evaluate against CAA and K-Steering (§6): PIXEL is limited to a single attribute with sample-specific calibration, while MAT-STEER, MSRS, and CST each require training composition-specific parameters tied to a fixed attribute set. An extended discussion is provided in Appendix A.

3.3 Angular Steering 

Angular Steering [Vu and Nguyen, 2025] recasts single-attribute steering as a rotation within a 2D subspace spanned by the feature direction ˆv and the activation’s orthogonal residual. The rotation angle controls steering strength continuously, the activation norm is preserved exactly, and the framework subsumes CAA (small angle ≈ additive perturbation) and directional ablation ( θ = π) as special cases. It is training-free but limited to a single attribute. The question we address is: how should K such rotations be composed without interference?

## 4 Method: ORBIT 

4.1 Motivation and core geometric insight 

Angular Steering provides a principled, norm-preserving rotation for a single attribute, but extending this framework to multiple attributes is nontrivial: independently estimated directions can conflict, making naive composition increasingly unstable as K grows. ORBIT resolves this by replacing separate local operations with a single rotation in a joint attribute subspace, preserving the orthogonal complement exactly while remaining fully training-free (no additional training or retraining when attributes change). Empirically, this unified formulation yields more balanced control than naive composition baselines (Appendix H), with results in §6. 

4.2 The ORBIT algorithm 

At a high level, ORBIT has a setup stage and a decoding stage: it prepares per-attribute planes in setup, then during decoding computes per-attribute misalignment gates, forms a target direction from the active attributes, rotates within the resulting steering subspace (which may reduce to a single-attribute plane), and reconstructs the full activation. Figure 2 illustrates this pipeline for the representative case K = 2 ; the same sequence applies to any K. Unless noted otherwise, we use one fixed configuration across tasks, and the additive boost is an optional post-rotation component.

α calibration. For CAA and K-Steering, α is calibrated per attribute combination via binary search: the largest value maintaining coherence is selected [Oozeer et al., 2025]. ## 6 Results 

Steering effectiveness. Table 1 summarizes judge scores, coherence, and geometric gain across all models, datasets, and values of K. ORBIT achieves the highest judge score in the majority of configurations and maintains coherence within a few points of the unsteered baseline, whereas CAA consistently degrades coherence. The advantage is most visible on T RAIT FACTORY , where safety-adjacent traits such as deception and evil resist additive perturbations: ORBIT outperforms the next-best score by 1.5× at K=1 and the margin widens as K grows. K-Steering scores near baseline on T RAIT FACTORY at K≥2, as its gradient signal is largely absorbed due to instruction-tuning alignment. The advantage also holds against the trained-composition method M AT -S TEER at a fixed attribute set (Appendix F.2) and persists at K=4 and K=5 (Appendix F.3). 

Balanced multi-attribute control. The geometric gain columns expose a failure mode hidden by mean scores: attribute dominance, where one trait improves at the expense of others. ORBIT is the only method that maintains positive geometric gain at every configuration, confirming that all targeted attributes improve simultaneously. CAA and K-Steering frequently turn negative at 

K≥2; K-Steering’s gain on T RAIT FACTORY K=3 is essentially zero across all models, while ORBIT remains between +0 .61 and +1 .22 . Joint success rates (Appendix F.1) corroborate this: on TRAIT FACTORY K=2 , ORBIT achieves 81% on Llama-3.2-3B versus 23% for K-Steering. The complementary minimum per-attribute improvement min k ∆k (Appendix F.4) confirms that even the weakest attribute improves under ORBIT, while it degrades under the baselines. Naive geometric composition variants also fail to steer more than one attribute (Appendix H).

## 7 Discussion 

Why rotation outperforms addition on resistant traits. Additive injections compound in magnitude across layers and degrade coherence, so in practice they are applied at only a few layers. Rotation, being norm-preserving, can instead be applied at every layer. We think this sustained, norm-stable intervention is why ORBIT steers resistant traits like deception and evil that CAA and K-Steering barely move. 

Computational cost. The cost of ORBIT is roughly twice the cost of CAA and an order of magnitude cheaper than K-Steering, which requires per-token backpropagation through its classifier (Appendix B). 

Limitations. Similar to prior contrastive methods, ORBIT represents each attribute with a linear steering direction, so attributes whose activation structure is strongly non-linear may not be fully captured by this approximation. On the evaluation side, although we steer up to K=5 attributes (§6, Appendix F.3), scaling to substantially larger attribute sets is left to future work. 

## 8 Conclusion 

We presented ORBIT, a training-free method for steering multiple behavioral attributes at inference time. Rather than summing per-attribute vectors, ORBIT constructs a single joint subspace via SVD and applies one norm-preserving rotation within it, guided by per-token adaptive gating. This design avoids the cancellation, norm inflation, and order dependence that destabilize naive composition as the number of attributes grows. Across two benchmarks, three models, and up to K=5 attributes, ORBIT consistently outperforms baselines on judge scores, geometric gain, and joint success rate while preserving coherence and general capabilities; through signed weights, it further supports antagonistic control, raising one attribute while suppressing another. Because it requires no training and generalizes to new attribute combinations without retraining, ORBIT offers a simple, flexible, and powerful mechanism for multi-attribute behavioral control.

## A Extended related work 

A.1 From alignment to inference-time control 

The dominant approach to shaping LLM behavior, RLHF [Ouyang et al., 2022, Bai et al., 2022a], Constitutional AI [Bai et al., 2022b], and instruction tuning, bakes a fixed behavioral profile into model weights. This is powerful but rigid: the resulting model cannot easily shift between behavioral modes at deployment time. Worse, fine-tuning on new tasks can erode existing alignment [Qi et al., 2023] or cause catastrophic forgetting [Luo et al., 2025]. These limitations motivate inference-time 

alternatives that modify behavior without updating parameters. 11 A.2 Single-attribute activation steering 

ROME [Meng et al., 2022] demonstrated that rank-one weight modifications can edit factual associa-tions, establishing that behavioral information is localized in activation space. PPLM [Dathathri et al., 2019] introduced gradient-guided activation steering via an attribute classifier, but requires per-token gradient computation and a trained classifier. Contrastive methods reduced the overhead. Activation Addition [ActAdd; Turner et al., 2023] extracts a steering vector from a single contrastive prompt pair and adds it at one layer. Inference-Time Intervention [ITI; Li et al., 2023] identifies informative attention heads via logistic regression probes; Representation Engineering [RepE; Zou et al., 2023] extracts feature directions through PCA over contrastive embeddings. CAA [Panickssery et al., 2023] computes a difference-of-means vector over contrastive pairs, added to activations with a single scalar coefficient. AXBench [Wu et al., 2025] showed that such linear baselines can match or outperform sparse autoencoders for single-attribute steering [Wang et al., 2025, Shu et al., 2025]; the reliability of contrastive directions has also been studied directly [Tan et al., 2024]. Geometric methods formulate steering in terms of activation-space transformations. Directional ablation [Arditi et al., 2024] removes a behavioral direction via Householder reflection. Angular Steering [Vu and Nguyen, 2025] generalizes this by formulating steering as a continuous, norm-preserving rotation in a 2D subspace, with CAA (small angle) and directional ablation ( θ = π) as special cases. Spherical Steering [You et al., 2026] extends this with geodesic interpolation and confidence-based gating. All of these methods operate on a single attribute. 

A.3 Non-linear probing and gradient-based steering 

Evidence that activation space has non-linear structure [Li et al., 2022, Hoscilowicz et al., 2024] suggests that linear methods may leave performance on the table. Zhang et al. [2025] showed that non-linear probe gradients can steer model behavior, a technique that K-Steering [Oozeer et al., 2025] formalized into a multi-label gradient-guided framework with an MLP classifier. These approaches capture richer representational structure but require training, creating a tension between expressiveness and flexibility. 

A.4 Multi-attribute composition 

The multi-attribute problem has received growing attention. van der Weij et al. [2024] conducted the first systematic study and concluded that naive vector addition for multiple attributes is “largely unsuccessful”, a finding that directly motivates our work. 

Additive multi-attribute composition. CAA [Panickssery et al., 2023] sums per-attribute mean-difference vectors with a scalar coefficient. K-Steering [Oozeer et al., 2025] avoids linearity through gradient guidance but ties the classifier to a fixed attribute configuration and requires classifier training. CONFST [Song et al., 2025] selects per-attribute vectors via Bayesian confidence and averages them, again a linear combination. These methods compose additively, making them susceptible to norm imbalance and directional cancellation as K grows. 

Multi-attribute steering with learned composition. MAT-STEER [Nguyen et al., 2025] addresses multi-attribute conflicts through learned per-token gating functions and orthogonality regularization, requiring a gradient-based training phase. MSRS [Jiang et al., 2025] decomposes multi-attribute steering into shared and attribute-specific subspaces via SVD, then learns projection and gating parameters through representation fine-tuning. Compositional Steering Tokens [Radevski et al., 2026] learns explicit composition operators via self-distillation, requiring training for each attribute combination. 

Per-sample adaptive steering. PIXEL [Yu et al., 2025] computes sample-specific steering vectors and adaptive scaling at inference time, achieving fine-grained per-input control at the cost of additional forward passes per sample. This operates along a different axis, input-dependent intensity, whereas ORBIT addresses the orthogonal challenge of composing multiple attributes without directional interference. 12 Table 2: Inference cost per generated token for multi-attribute steering methods. d is the hidden dimension, K the number of attributes, m ≤ 2K the subspace dimension, S the number of gradient steps, and D the classifier hidden dimension. ## B Theoretical properties and complexity 

Reduction to Angular Steering for K = 1 . When K = 1 , the basis matrix V = [ ˆb1, ˆb2] has rank 2, and the SVD produces E = [ e1, e2] spanning the same 2D plane. The target reduces to 

t = ˆb1, and the rotation within E is exactly the Angular Steering rotation. ORBIT is thus a strict generalization: it adds multi-attribute capability without sacrificing single-attribute behavior. 

Behavior across inter-attribute geometries. The subspace dimension m provides a lens into how ORBIT handles different geometric configurations. When attribute directions are mutually orthogonal, m = 2 K and the rotation simultaneously aligns with all attributes independently, the ideal case where no trade-offs are needed. When directions are correlated (e.g., “expert” and “formal” share a high cosine similarity), m < 2K and the SVD automatically merges their shared variance, effectively steering a reduced set of independent factors. When directions oppose, the adaptive gating resolves the conflict: per-token gate strengths gk weight each attribute by its actual misalignment, so the combined target t naturally prioritizes whichever attributes are currently under-represented in the activation, in contrast to parallel sum, which blindly sums opposing deltas.
