# Adaptive Platt Scaling with Causal Interpretations for Self-Reflective Language Model Uncertainty Estimates

## Abstract 

As large language models (LLMs) are con-sumed by more users and deployed in increas-ingly autonomous capacities, their ability to self-monitor and ask for human intervention is of vital importance. Underlying this capability are fundamental skills like self-reflection and expression of uncertainty. In this work, we pro-vide a formal analysis of LLM self-reflection for uncertainty estimation, using domain adap-tation theory to model the shift between base predictions and reflective judgments. We use this to motivate a temperature scaling algo-rithm that calibrates uncertainty using com-parisons between base predictions and LLM self-reflections. We evaluate our approach on challenging question-answering tasks requiring reasoning, demonstrating that our methods can improve calibration of uncertainty estimates and also offer improvements in human inter-pretation. More broadly, this use case shows how domain adaptation presents a promising analytical tool for understanding the underlying statistical properties of LLM self-reflections. 

## 1 Introduction 

As LLMs are deployed to more users and given greater autonomy, their ability to self-monitor gains increasing importance. Models need to recognize when they might be wrong and externalize this un-certainty in interpretable ways, reducing users’ cog-nitive loads to enable reliable LLM use (Tankele-vitch et al., 2024). For example, this is especially true in safety-critical settings like healthcare (Guo et al., 2017). Despite this, we still lack a clear un-derstanding of how LLM self-monitoring works, how to interpret it, and how to use it for reliable uncertainty estimation. Existing methods primarily treat model reflections and uncertainty estimates as static, isolated outputs, overlooking the dynamic

Our work addresses this gap by introducing a theoretically-grounded method, which recognizes LLM self-reflections as a source of dynamic, in-terpretable uncertainty. Specifically, we propose a new calibration algorithm that leverages self-reflective outputs to improve the accuracy of un-certainty estimates, comparing these to base pre-dictions during Platt Scaling (Platt et al., 1999). By formalizing self-reflection through the lens of domain adaptation (Ben-David et al., 2010), we explicitly account for changes between initial pre-dictions and reflective judgments, framing LLM self-reflections as a form of distribution shift. Our method is a practical, lightweight instan-tiation of our theoretical inquiry that can be im-plemented via a single additional regression layer, incurring negligible computational costs to typical self-reflection workflows. Interestingly, we also demonstrate how this technique can offer improved human interpretation, framing the technique as a causal analysis for when LLMs may be more likely to make errors. We apply our methods to a range of question-answering tasks with a focus on reason-ing, challenging LLM self-reflections to recognize when reasoning may be uncertain or incomplete. Domains include logical tasks; difficult problems in topics like Science, Business, or History (Suzgun et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024); and tasks requiring reasoning about the future (Sicilia et al., 2024). To summarize, our contributions include: 1. we introduce a domain adaptation framework for analyzing LLM self-reflections; 2. we use our analytical tool to propose an uncer-tainty estimation method that improves cali-bration by using reflective outputs; 3. we suggest an associated method for interpre-tation, drawing connection to causal analysis; and, we publish code to enable future applications. 1

Problem Setup We focus on expressing uncer-tainty as a basic form of self-monitoring capability. In this setting, a language model is presented with a random query Q and generates an answer A, with the goal of estimating the probability that A is cor-rect. Basic Approach Uncertainty estimation methods for language models typically involve extracting a model-derived signal correlated with correctness, then calibrating this signal to output a probability (Kadavath et al., 2022). We focus on Direct Fore-casts , where the model is prompted to directly ex-press confidence via tokens in its generated output (e.g., a number 1–10). ## 3 Proposed Methods 

Self-Reflection We use self-reflection to broadly describe any prompting technique that asks lan-guage models to re-consider , reflect on , correct ,

revise , etc. their own previously generated answer (Shinn et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2024). For example, this strat-egy would fall under post-hoc self-feedback in the survey by Pan et al. (2024) and is distinct from strategies that use reflection to improve de-coding (Yang et al., 2022; Weng et al., 2023) or fine-tune a model (Huang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). Under this broad umbrella, with a focus on uncertainty estimation, we will assume the re-prompted lan-guage model generates a new string R conditional to the pre-existing dialogue QA , and then a new uncertainty signal ˆZQAR is formed. A simplified example is given below: 

Q : What is 42 plus 111? 

A : ...so it’s 156, I’m 70% sure. 

user : Re-analyze & report confidence. 

R : ...due to this, I’m only 40% sure. 

Here, ˆZQA = 70% and ˆZQAR = 40% . We report our precise prompt for reflection, examples, and other prompts for our experiments in the Appendix.

## 4 Experiments 

Models We use Meta’s Llama 3.1 with 8B param-eters and Llama 4 Scout (Touvron et al., 2023). We also use Google’s Gemma 2 with 9B parameters (Team et al., 2024) and Mistral Small 3. We access models via Together AI and set temperature = 0.7. 

Datasets We use three reasoning benchmarks: 1. BBH , short for Big Bench Hard, is a com-monly used suite of logical tasks proposed by Suzgun et al. (2023) to test models’ reasoning capabilities. The data meta-labels ( d) are the different problem subtypes within the data. 2. MMLU -Pro, is a question-answering suite proposed by Wang et al. (2024). It is based on the MMLU benchmark (Hendrycks et al., 2020), but increases difficulty to stress-test reasoning. Meta-labels are problem domains; e.g., Business, History, Physics, etc. 3. FD , short for FortUneDial, is a suite of tasks proposed by Sicilia et al. (2024) to test reason-ing about future events during conversation; e.g., predicting if a deal will occur at the end of a negotiation. Meta-labels are conversation domains; e.g., negotiation, online forum, etc. 

Optimization We estimate coefficients for Platt Scaling using default Logit hyper-parameters in the statsmodels package. We use a consistent 60/40 train/test split of 1200 points from each dataset.

Notably, by their definition, the causal effects we study are not accounted for during LLM self-reflection, allowing practitioners added information about potential model failures. 18417 5 Discussion 

Our experiments across BBH, MMLU-Pro, and FortUneDial show that reflective calibration (Eq. 9) consistently improves Brier Skill Score, with gains that are statistically significant in several cases. While improvements are sometimes modest in mag-nitude, they are robust across domains, and, impor-tantly, come from an extremely lightweight and simple methodolgical choice: an added regression layer with (at most) tens of parameters. While beneficial in most cases, increasing the number of learned parameters inherently impacts the vari-ance of performance (i.e., there is no free lunch, Wolpert and Macready, 2002). This is evidenced by increased dispersion between the min and max model performance in Table 1. 

Practical Considerations Generally speaking, the method is easy to implement in real-world pipelines. It requires only two ingredients that prac-titioners already have access to: 1. model confidence signals , which can be either direct forecasts verbalized by the LLM (e.g., I’m 70% sure ) or logit-based scores for the model responses – we study the former in § 4, but other works have succesfully used logit-based scores during calibration (Sicilia et al., 2024); 2. a single regression model , which requires fit-ting a logistic model with interaction terms by numerical MLE, using hundreds of data points. With these in hand, inference requires one addi-tional reflection prompt, doubling the number of forward passes for the LLM, which is the clear computational bottleneck. Thus, the total overhead is small compared to common LLM costs and neg-ligible if added to an existing reflection workflow. 

The Impact of Scale While we evaluated medium-scale LLMs on relatively small data sources, we expect scaling either of these dimen-sions would preserve our findings. In past work, larger models maintain consistent performance gains during both self-reflection (Madaan et al., 2023) and uncertainty calibration (Sicilia et al., 2024), so we do not expect our combination of these methodologies to present significantly differ-ent findings. The fact that the calibrating regres-sion layer is independent of model size strengthens this hypothesis. With respect to data, we observe the (internal) diversity of the datasets we consider, whose subsets span law, engineering, health, his-tory, social dialogues, task-oriented dialogues, etc. Our statistically significant results on these varied data provide evidence that our method should re-main applicable to other data and tasks. 

Interpretation Gains Beyond raw calibration improvements, our causal framing provides inter-pretability not offered by other methods. For ex-ample, in MMLU-Pro we were able to identify that computer science and law questions pose larger systematic challenges to LLMs than history or psy-chology. On average, a model will do worse on these domains, speaking to the contexts where a practitioner can trust their model outputs. This intuition is also precisely quantified through the regression layer’s coefficients, which allowed us to rank domain difficulty in our case study. Other po-tential applications could utilize these coefficients in LLM interfaces to dynamically warn users about potential errors, use them in training pipelines to re-fine data (e.g., as a form of active learning), or use them in myriad model diagnostics, such as longitu-dinal studies of performance across model versions. Compared to raw performance metrics split by data, our regression coefficients attempt to control for potential confounders – these would typically be commonalities across data domains (e.g., the pres-ence of equations in both computer science and physics problems) that can bias interpretation of traditional performance metrics. Lastly, we note that even when performance drops slightly (e.g., on FortUneDial with Mistral Small), the interpretive benefits remain. A practitioner can always revert to simpler calibration strategies if performance de-creases, but retain our method for interpretability. 

## 6 Conclusion 

This work provides theory and conclusive empirical evidence to motivate a new reflective calibration algorithm that improves LLM uncertainty estimates and enables causal explanation of model errors. Both come at negligible computational cost. However, beyond lightweight diagnostic tooling, our contributions might be viewed as an entryway into rethinking how LLMs engage with their own understanding. The very act of asking a model to reconsider is not a simple a calibration trick, but a way to alter reasoning. Indeed, our experiments suggest that reflection alters the model’s reasoning distribution itself, uncovering uncertainty signals that static inference obscures. This suggests reflec-tive uncertainty may function as a core element, and design lever, for multi-step LLM reasoning. 18418 Limitations 

Despite promising empirical results and a backing theory, it is important to recognize the assumptions of our theory may not hold for all models/datasets. As observed in our results, an inherent pitfall of our approach is the possibility of poor generalization – induced by the increased sample-complexity of our calibration approach – which can actually lead to 

worse performance in certain circumstances. For-tunately, we observe these problematic situations are rare empirically and our formal framing does makes all assumptions explicit, enabling future re-searchers to improve upon these and build methods that cover broader application settings. On a related note, it should be observed that self-reflection and expression of LLM uncertainty still has a significant room for improvements. In general, our results are on par with other LLM un-certainty estimation literature (Sicilia et al., 2024), but comparison of our best uncertainty estimation results (BBH) and the most similar benchmark (MMLU) shows a gap of about 6.5% explained variance. Further research is needed to design meth-ods which can improve across difficult application domains, and we hope our theory can provide a principled backbone for this work. 

## Ethics Statement 

While the methods we propose are designed to alert practitioners of potential pitfalls in LLM pre-dictions, these techniques should not be treated as a catchall AI safety mechanism. The models and methods we study have known (and unknown) biases that can cause direct and indirect harm to individuals if their outputs are incorporated in au-tomatic processes, decisions, or content generation without careful human supervision. The models and methods presented here should be treated as research artifacts that are not yet intended for de-ployment in real-world applications. Instead, any real-world deployment should be assessed for po-tential negative impacts and paired with holistic safety mechanisms to mitigate harm. For example, these mechanisms should incorporate rigorous sta-tistical study, as in the current paper, but also utilize careful, human-centered co-design, incorporating direct and indirect stakeholders to ensure systems that benefit the public good.
