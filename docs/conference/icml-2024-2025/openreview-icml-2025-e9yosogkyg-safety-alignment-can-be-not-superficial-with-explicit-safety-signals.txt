## Abstract 

Recent studies on the safety alignment of large language models (LLMs) have revealed that exist-ing approaches often operate superficially, leaving models vulnerable to various adversarial attacks. Despite their significance, these studies gener-ally fail to offer actionable solutions beyond data augmentation for achieving more robust safety mechanisms. This paper identifies a fundamen-tal cause of this superficiality: existing alignment approaches often presume that models can implic-itly learn a safety-related reasoning task during the alignment process, enabling them to refuse harmful requests. However, the learned safety signals are often diluted by other competing ob-jectives, leading models to struggle with draw-ing a firm safety-conscious decision boundary when confronted with adversarial attacks. Based on this observation, by explicitly introducing a safety-related binary classification task and inte-grating its signals with our attention and decoding strategies, we eliminate this ambiguity and allow models to respond more responsibly to malicious queries. We emphasize that, with less than 0.2x overhead cost, our approach enables LLMs to assess the safety of both the query and the previ-ously generated tokens at each necessary generat-ing step. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method significantly improves the resilience of LLMs against various adversarial attacks, of-fering a promising pathway toward more robust generative AI systems.

Aligned with Li & Kim (2024), we interpret an aligned model’s ability to correctly identify malicious queries as an implicit binary classification task. However, we observed a critical hurdle: this implicit safety signal is often diluted or overridden by competing objectives, such as learning complex human preferences regarding tone, style, or phras-ing of responses. This entanglement between safety and general objectives not only makes it difficult to interpret the influence of each but also leads to uncertainty in the forma-tion process of the model’s decision when facing complex adversarial queries. We verified this with the top-K log-its at each decoding step, which exhibits significant model confusion when confronted with adversarial attacks, further highlighting the limitations of relying on implicit safety rea-soning signals in such scenarios. A similar observation is also evidenced by Xu et al. (2024b). Based on the observations above, we propose incorporating an explicit safety-related binary classification task into the model training process. Specifically, inspired by BERT, we introduce a special [CLS] token at the beginning of every input sequence. The output corresponding to this token is used to classify the input or context as benign or malicious. This integration can effectively prevent the dilution of safety-related signals compared to the previous implicit ways. Moreover, we design two novel mechanisms to fully lever-age the extracted explicit signals in the deployment phase: 

1) Strategic Attention Mechanism: This mechanism inte-grates the hidden state of the [CLS] token into the model’s generative process, allowing it to implicitly incorporate safety signals during text generation. 2) Strategic Decod-ing Strategy: This strategy explicitly leverages the predic-tion of the binary classification task to guide the model’s decision-making process during text generation, enabling it to respond to complex adversarial scenarios more timely and confidently. These enhancements allow LLMs to move beyond reliance on implicit safety reasoning signals embed-ded in the generation process and instead adopt a structured approach to extract and utilize explicit safety signals. Furthermore, to tackle adversarial scenarios, we extend the use of the [CLS] token to the entire decoding process. In particular, at only necessary generation steps, the model re-infers the [CLS] token to reassess the original input query as well as the tokens generated so far . This real-time reassessment mechanism enables the model to dynamically verify the safety of its current reasoning trajectory on the fly 

and, if necessary, re-select the correct reasoning direction to avoid generating harmful content. Importantly, this sim-ple extension ensures that the model can defend against a wide range of adversarial attacks as long as it can reliably classify the input and previously generated tokens as benign or malicious. Since binary classification is a relatively sim-ple task compared to the implicit safety reasoning required by LLMs, this design significantly reduces the difficulty of achieving robust safety alignment. Notably, we introduce multiple selective re-evaluation strategies to ensure compu-tational efficiency, which minimizes additional inference costs while maintaining consistent safety performance. We have code implementation and other information on the project website: https://sa-ess.github.io/ .

## 3. Methodology 

This section first introduces how the binary classification task is integrated in our approach. Then, we detail the two key components of the design: a strategic attention mecha-nism, which implicitly leverages the safety-related signals embedded in the hidden states of the binary classification task, and a strategic decoding strategy, which explicitly uti-lizes the binary classification output to guide the generation process. These two components together enable the model to address safety challenges more effectively. 

3.1. Binary Classification Task - Explicit Safety Signals 

Generative LLMs are primarily designed to predict the next token using a causal attention mechanism, while these mod-els can also handle classification tasks by appending a head to the output of the last token. However, this approach has potential risks when simultaneously optimizing both genera-tion and classification objectives on the same token because it may leads to suboptimal performance of each. Inspired by the architecture of BERT (Devlin, 2018), which is tailored for classification tasks, we introduce a dedicated classifica-tion token [CLS] at the beginning of each input sequence to address this issue. The [CLS] token’s output is then passed through a specific classification head to classify whether the input and the tokens generated so far are safe or unsafe. As illustrated in Fig. 1, this design can separate the objective of binary classification from the next token prediction to optimize them based on different token information. 

Challenge A.1: Balancing Classification with Generative Capabilities. When introducing the binary classification task, one challenge is ensuring that it does not compromise the model’s generative capability. While we optimize the new objective based on a specific token, it still has a risk of hurting the model’s original function. To address this issue, we apply the following three specific rules in the training phase: 1) In pretraining , we integrate the binary classifi-cation task early in the pretraining process. This allows the base model to gradually adapt to the classification task with-out disrupting its primary generative ability. Importantly, we carefully control the attention mechanism: the [CLS] 

token is allowed to attend to all other tokens, but other to-kens do not attend to the [CLS] token (Fig. 1). This ensures that the original causal attention logic remains intact. 2) In alignment , such as supervised fine-tuning (SFT), we further isolate the classification and generation tasks by constrain-ing the attention flow. 3.2. Strategic Attention Design - Implicit Leverage 

To leverage the safety-related signals introduced by the bi-nary classification task, a very straightforward approach is to use the attention mechanism in the Transformer architecture to allow the hidden state of the [CLS] token to influence the newly generated tokens. Ideally, the model, during the generation of response tokens, should be able to detect nuanced changes in the hidden state of the [CLS] token. When harmful features accumulate to a certain threshold, the model should immediately stop generating, refuse the user’s malicious request, and provide a justification for the refusal. However, achieving this ideal outcome requires addressing multiple challenges, as outlined below: 

Challenge B.1: Accuracy of Intermediate Features of the 

[CLS] Token. The intermediate features of the [CLS] 

token need to be accurate and clearly distinguished between benign and malicious. However, this distinction is not di-rectly observable. Fortunately, if the prediction label of the [CLS] token is sufficiently accurate, we can infer that its intermediate features must exhibit such distinctions at certain Transformer layers. This transforms the problem into ensuring the classification label’s accuracy, but leading to the second challenge. 

Challenge B.2: Dynamic Re-evaluation of the [CLS] 

Token during Generation. The inference process of the 

[CLS] token is not static. To continuously keep assessing the safety of the input and the already generated tokens throughout the generation process, it is necessary to dynami-cally re-evaluate the safety of the context at each generation step or at selective steps (to determine when re-evaluations should occur, we conduct Ablation Study 3 in Sec. 4.3.) To achieve dynamic assessment, we consider three more nuanced rules to address specific scenarios, as illustrated in Fig. 2: Rule 1) If the [CLS] token initially classifies the query as malicious , it does not need to attend to all newly generated tokens. Instead, we allow the [CLS] token to 4Safety Alignment Can Be Not Superficial With Explicit Safety Signals 

attend only to the input tokens and the initial r1 generated tokens. Rule 2) If the [CLS] token initially classifies the query as benign , the [CLS] token attends exclusively to the latest r2 generated tokens until its prediction switches to malicious. The rationale behind this rule is threefold: to fo-cus more on newer generated content while de-emphasizing the query; to enable model to operate accurately within a properly limited context; to save computational cost. Rule 3) If the [CLS] token’s output is initially classified as be-nign but some steps later converted to malicious (indicating the presence of harmful content), we record this step as a transition point, St. At this point, we adjust the [CLS] 

token’s attention to focus on the tokens within the range 

[St − r2, S t + r3] (see Fig. 2). This adjustment provides the model a fault tolerance, preventing misclassification caused by overly sensitive triggers such as specific harm-ful keywords (in such cases, the system automatically falls back to Rule 2 for continued safety assessment). 4) The 

[CLS] token’s attention should skip auxiliary tokens, such as padding (PAD), start-of-sequence (BOS), and instruc-tion tokens like “ Please answer the following instruction. ”Please note that r1, r2, and r3 are hyperparameters that can be determined according to different scenarios and practical requirements, enabling flexible control over the attention mechanism. More details are in Appendix D.2. 

Challenge B.3: Measuring the Impact of [CLS] Token Information. The final challenge is determining whether the information provided by the [CLS] token has suffi-ciently influenced the model to alter its original reasoning direction and refuse to generate harmful content. Since the influence of attention mechanisms is often subtle and lim-ited, we design Ablation Study 4 in Sec. 4.3 to investigate the effectiveness of the [CLS] token. 

3.3. Strategic Decoding Strategy - Explicit Leverage 

The previously discussed strategic attention mechanism leverages the hidden state of the [CLS] token to implicitly influence the text generation process. However, since this mechanism relies on the accumulation of nuanced safety signals, its effects are inherently uncontrollable, and it pro-vides no clear way to determine the exact moment to shift the model’s safety reasoning direction. This lack of control severely affects the reliability of the model’s safety mech-anism. An alternative approach is explicitly utilizing the classification output of the [CLS] token. As in Fig. 3, there are three levels of reliance on the classification prediction: (1) Complete Reliance on Attention (low reliance): The classification prediction is ignored, and the model re-lies solely on the strategic attention mechanism. (2) Immediate Termination (high reliance): The model halts the generation process immediately once the clas-sification prediction is malicious, and the system out-puts a fixed response, such as, “ Sorry, I cannot fulfill your request. ” While this approach is simple and di-rect, it may result in high false positives , where benign queries are unnecessarily refused. This method also lacks the ability to provide a justification of the refusal. (3) Strategic Decoding (Our Approach) (mid reliance): To balance the trade-offs between safety, simplicity, and interpretability, we propose a middle ground. This approach explicitly utilizes the classification label dur-ing the decoding process, with the following rules: 1) 

If the model initially classifies the query as malicious, it immediately inserts guiding tokens, such as, “ Sorry, I cannot fulfill your request because, ” and then pro-ceeds to explain the reason for refusal. 2) If the query is initially classified as benign but changed to mali-cious during the generation process and this malicious classification persists for τ consecutive steps, the same guiding token is inserted at the point of transition. Our strategy is also justified by the following observations: 

Addressing Decision Boundary Uncertainty : In adver-sarial queries, where the malicious intent is concealed, the classification probabilities of the [CLS] token often hover near the decision boundary (e.g., around 0.5.) This uncer-tainty causes hesitation in the model’s reasoning, making it difficult for the attention mechanism alone to respond effec-tively. At this critical point, the decoding strategy intervenes explicitly, enabling the model to make a decisive shift in its safety reasoning and act promptly. 

Incorporating Chain-of-Thought for Justification : When inserting the guiding token, we intentionally adopt a chain-of-thought approach (Wei et al., 2022), where the model not only refuses to fulfill the request but also provides a reasoning process to explain why it cannot comply. This design enhances the model’s understanding of both the query and the already-generated tokens while also reducing the likelihood of the model reversing its decision. Moreover, this human-readable justification for refusals minimizes the risk of ending up with false positives/false negatives.

## 5. Discussion 

CLS vs. SFT vs. DPO vs. RLHF. Compared to traditional alignment methods like SFT, DPO, and RLHF, our approach introduces minimal additional training requirements while achieving robust safety alignment. Specifically, our method does not require preference data (as in DPO) or extensive sampling processes (as in RLHF). Unlike RLHF, which re-quires training and maintaining both a reward model and a reference model, our approach retains the simplicity of single-model fine-tuning by only adding a lightweight bi-nary classification task. This streamlined design not only reduces computational overhead but also makes our method easier to train and update in response to emerging attacks. Moreover, our method can be applied as a post-alignment enhancement stage to strengthen the safety guarantees estab-lished by existing techniques such as SFT, DPO, or RLHF. More details of comparison with DPO are in Appendix B. 

Effectiveness Against GCG and Decoding Attacks. One significant takeaway is the outstanding effectiveness of our approach against gradient-based attacks like GCG. These attacks rely on optimized adversarial suffixes to produce predefined harmful responses. However, the dynamic re-classification mechanism fundamentally disrupts this pro-cess. By continuously updating the hidden state of the 

[CLS] token, our method breaks the static adversarial sig-nal introduced by the appended suffix, on which GCG op-timizations rely, rendering the attack strategy ineffective. Moreover, we observe that our method is not sensitive to sampling, as it consistently demonstrates near-zero standard deviation across multiple rounds of generation. As shown in Tab. 1 & 2, this stability is also supported by its superiority in defending against decoding attacks. 

Cost Analysis. During the training phase, we introduce one additional token, resulting in negligible computational overhead. In the deployment phase, the strategic attention mechanism ensures that the [CLS] token focuses only on relevant subsets of tokens rather than attending to all to-kens. Moreover, as demonstrated in Ablation Study 3 in Sec. 4.3, selective reclassification strategies further reduce inference costs by limiting safety checks to the necessary generation steps. Together, these two strategies effectively minimize computational overhead. Notably, regardless of the phase, our method does not increase computation time, as it operates in parallel with existing strategies. 

Limitation and Future work This paper proposes a novel approach to enhance LLM’s safety by integrating a safety-related binary classification task with strategic attention and decoding mechanisms. However, our current study has several limitations. First, we mainly focus on text-based adversarial attacks; extending the method to multimodal systems remains an important direction for future work. Second, the pretraining phase—while conceptually bene-ficial—did not yield substantial performance gains in our experiments. We attribute this to the use of weak supervi-sion from existing safety models such as Llama3-Guard ,which may introduce label noise. Furthermore, due to lim-ited computational resources, we were unable to scale up pretraining with higher-quality annotations. Addressing this issue through improved labeling and larger-scale pretraining would be an exciting avenue for future exploration. 

## 6. Conclusion 

This paper proposes a novel approach to enhance LLM safety alignment by integrating an explicit binary classifica-tion task with strategic attention and decoding mechanisms. These components implicitly or explicitly leverage safety-related signals to ensure reliable responses and improve robustness against adversarial and jailbreak attacks. 

## Impact Statement 

The methods proposed in this paper aim to address criti-cal challenges in improving the safety alignment of LLMs. These advancements reduce the risk of harmful outputs, increase interpretability, and improve user trust in gener-ative AI systems, which is especially important as LLMs are increasingly integrated into sensitive domains such as education, healthcare, and customer service.

## Appendix A. Relation to Superficial Safety Alignment Hypothesis 

A.1. Superficial Safety Alignment Hypothesis 

The Superficial Safety Alignment Hypothesis (SSAH) provides a theoretical framework for understanding how existing safety alignment techniques impact the behavior of Large Language Models (LLMs) under direct attack scenarios (Li & Kim, 2024). It builds upon the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis (SAH) , which posits that alignment processes primarily teach models to adopt specific subdistributions of output formats (Zhou et al., 2024). However, SSAH specifically focuses on safety alignment, differentiating itself in scope, objectives, and mechanisms. SSAH is grounded in a critical observation: A model that can fulfill malicious requests must already possess the necessary 

knowledge and reasoning capabilities . Based on this premise, SSAH hypothesizes that existing safety alignment methods serve two primary functions: (1) Teaching the Correct Reasoning Direction : Safety alignment steers the model’s internal decision-making process, enabling it to select the correct reasoning direction when confronted with malicious inputs. This decision-making process can be interpreted as a binary classification task —either to fulfill the request or to issue a refusal. (2) Embedding Standardized Refusal Mechanisms : Unlike general alignment, safety alignment often requires the model to provide relatively standardized refusal responses for unsafe queries, such as: • “I cannot fulfill your request as it violates safety guidelines.” 

• “I am unable to assist with that as I am programmed to follow ethical standards.” 

These refusal formats simplify the safety alignment problem by reducing variability in acceptable responses, as they eliminate the need to account for diverse stylistic choices, tonal differences, and nuanced variations in phrasing that are typically required in general alignment. By standardizing refusal patterns, the model can focus solely on ensuring safety compliance without being influenced by subjective factors such as politeness or formality. With this hypothesis, we can isolate safety alignment from broader factors, such as knowledge acquisition during pretraining or the complex subdistribution of output formats during general alignment, and focus specifically on how safety alignment influences the model’s behavior, providing a clear theoretical foundation for addressing LLM misuse and harmfulness. 

A.2. Robust Safety Alignment Hypothesis 

While SSAH primarily explains the theoretical role of safety alignment under direct attacks, it also indicates the need for a more robust framework, which here we referred to as the Robust Safety Alignment Hypothesis (RSAH) : Building upon SSAH, the model should be able to re-evaluate and re-route the reasoning direction at each generation step (Li & Kim, 2024). This hypothesis extends the principles of SSAH by addressing adversarial and jailbreak attack scenarios, where malicious queries attempt to bypass the model’s safety mechanisms. According to this hypothesis, the model can provide safety guardrails throughout text generation. 

A.3. Practical Implementation in Recent Work 

Recent work has explored various approaches to address the superficiality of existing safety alignment in large language models. Some of these approaches resonate with the principles of RSAH , although they fall short when applied to more intricate attack scenarios (Qi et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024). The insights provided by SSAH and RSAH also form the theoretical foundation of this paper. We achieve these hypotheses by introducing explicit safety signals through a [CLS] 

token and integrating dynamic re-evaluation and decoding strategies during the generation process. Specifically, the model leverages: (1) Strategic Attention Mechanism : This mechanism enables the hidden state of the [CLS] token to dynamically update and implicitly influence text generation. It adjusts the model’s reasoning direction in response to adversarial inputs. (2) Strategic Decoding Strategy : This strategy explicitly utilizes the binary classification prediction of the [CLS] token to guide and justify the model’s refusal responses. 13 Safety Alignment Can Be Not Superficial With Explicit Safety Signals 

Through these mechanisms, we address the limitations of existing safety alignment methods and extend their capabilities to handle adversarial and jailbreak attack scenarios. Our experimental results demonstrate that these practical implementations significantly improve the model’s robustness, achieving near-zero attack success rates across various benchmarks. In summary, SSAH explains the impact of current safety alignment techniques on model behavior under direct attacks, while RSAH provides a theoretical path forward to enhance robustness against more complex adversarial scenarios. Our work bridges these theoretical insights with practical implementations, advancing the state-of-the-art in safety alignment. 

## B. Comparison with Direct Preference Optimization 

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is a widely adopted approach that optimizes models using preference data, guiding them to favor chosen responses while avoiding rejected ones (Rafailov et al., 2024). Although DPO is effective in many scenarios, it differs fundamentally from our method in its approach to safety alignment across several dimensions, including dataset, purpose, methodology, side effects, complexity, and compatibility. Below, we outline the key distinctions: 

Binary Classification vs. Preference Data. DPO relies on preference data to optimize models iteratively, causing them to gradually lean toward desirable outputs. In contrast, our approach directly uses a binary classification dataset to train the model to identify whether an input-output pair is safe or unsafe. This explicit focus on safety allows the model to establish a clearer understanding of harmfulness, rather than relying on relative preferences. 

Dedicated Safety Focus vs. General Objectives. DPO is designed to handle a wide range of optimization tasks, such as aligning models to favor certain response formats, including formal vs. informal tone, factual vs. conversational styles, and others. While this flexibility is valuable, it dilutes its ability to specifically address safety concerns. Our approach, in comparison, narrows the objective to the safety domain. It can be seen as a subset of DPO, with a sharper focus on ensuring safety alignment. This specialization makes our method more targeted and efficient for safety-critical scenarios. 

Explicit Safe-related Signals vs. Relative Preference. DPO typically handles safety alignment in a relative manner, ranking one output as “safer” than another. However, this lack of explicit safe/unsafe signals introduces ambiguity, making models vulnerable to adversarial and jailbreak attacks. Our approach provides explicit signals for safety, enabling the model to distinguish between safe and unsafe outputs accurately and timely. This distinction significantly enhances robustness, especially in complex scenarios where harmful content is nested or emergent. 

Reasoning Capability: Restriction vs. Guidance. When addressing safety concerns, DPO often optimizes models to outright refuse queries associated with unsafe content. This approach is reflected in the labeling process, which tends to favor refusal over more nuanced responses. Similarly, our method also uses refusal as a default label in our training data. However, unlike DPO, our framework allows flexibility in relaxing this restriction when the context requires reasoning within unsafe content to provide more helpful responses. By solely relying on predictions of the binary classification task to guide the reasoning process, our approach avoids suppressing the model’s reasoning capabilities but also provides a safety guardrail. This advancement enables the model to maintain safety while retaining its capacity for more nuanced reasoning. 

Simplified Safety Alignment vs. Complex Distribution Tasks. By framing harmfulness as a straightforward binary classification problem, our approach separates it from the broader task of aligning with user preferences. This clear focus and the innovative abstraction into a binary classification task simplify the alignment process, making it more lightweight and easier to implement without sacrificing effectiveness. 

Compatibility with DPO. While our method embeds the binary classification task into the SFT process, it is inherently compatible with DPO as well. The classification mechanism can be seamlessly integrated into DPO tasks to enhance safety alignment, highlighting its flexibility and adaptability across different training paradigms. Importantly, when the [CLS] 

token is not included in the input, the model naturally reverts to the original alignment paradigm with which it was trained, whether it be an SFT finetuned model or a DPO fine-tuned model. More details can be found in Appendix D-1. In essence, DPO provides a powerful framework for aligning models to user preferences, but its generality introduces limitations when applied to safety-critical domains. Our method, with its explicit safe/unsafe signals, narrower focus, and lightweight implementation, complements DPO by addressing these gaps. Furthermore, the flexibility of the binary classification mechanism ensures it can be used alongside DPO, bridging the strengths of both approaches to advance the state of safety alignment. 14 Safety Alignment Can Be Not Superficial With Explicit Safety Signals 

## C. Detailed Setups of Our Experiments 

C.1. Compute Resources 

All experiments presented in this paper were carried out on a single machine, which was configured with three NVIDIA A6000 GPUs to handle the computationally intensive tasks, 256GB of memory to accommodate large-scale data processing and model training requirements, and 16 CPU cores to manage auxiliary operations. 

Compute Limitations. Due to the limitations of our computing resources, we could not validate our method on models larger than 10B parameters. For optimization-based attacks such as GCG and AutoDAN , the validation process was excessively time-consuming, which restricted us from conducting sufficient evaluations. In particular, when comparing with state-of-the-art baselines, we could not provide results for GCG-based attacks. However, as discussed in the Discussion 

section, we provide theoretical arguments to support the effectiveness of our method in these scenarios. We urge the community to provide greater support to groups with limited computing resources. Research contributions should not be dismissed solely due to the inability to validate results on larger models or datasets. Instead, we advocate for recognizing the theoretical and methodological merits of such work while fostering opportunities for further validation on larger models as resources become available. 

C.2. Decoding Parameters. 

Throughout our experiments, we employ top-p sampling as the default method for decoding outputs from large language models. Specifically, we utilize a temperature of 0.7 to introduce controlled randomness into the generation process and a top-p parameter of 0.9 to ensure that only the most probable tokens are sampled, thereby balancing diversity and coherence. This configuration is used consistently across all experiments except for the decoding attack experiment, where the experimental design deliberately manipulates these decoding parameters to evaluate potential vulnerabilities (Huang et al., 2023). Notably, our proposed method exhibits remarkable robustness against decoding attacks, even under varying sampling configurations. This resilience is achieved through the integration of binary classification predictions and the 

strategic decoding strategy , which collectively mitigate the influence of adversarial manipulations in decoding settings. Consequently, our approach remains largely insensitive to changes in sampling parameters. Further analysis for this observation is provided in the Sec. 5. 

C.3. Safety Evaluator A Call for Improved Evaluation Practices. We advocate for the community to stop relying on keyword-matching methods to compute Attack Success Rates (ASR). Given the increasing complexity of modern attacks, such simplistic methods are no longer sufficient. For instance, adversarial attacks can generate harmful content even after a model refuses the initial request by exploiting polluted training data or appending optimized suffixes. These tactics render keyword-matching-based evaluations meaningless in many scenarios.

C.4. Training Details 

The training phase in this paper involves two stages: Pretraining and Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT) . In fact, we introduced the pretraining phase only when enhancing the base model, whereas, for the aligned model, we solely applied supervised fine-tuning. Due to constraints in computing resources and data availability, we also acknowledge certain limitations below. 

Pretraining Phase. To preserve the reasoning capability of the base model during pretraining, the most reliable approach would be to distill the logits of the original model directly. This would ensure that introducing the binary classification task early in the training process does not negatively impact the model’s original outputs. Unfortunately, we could not implement 21 Safety Alignment Can Be Not Superficial With Explicit Safety Signals 

this approach due to the limited memory of CPU resources. Additionally, during pretraining, the maximum sequence length we used was restricted to 1024, which is shorter than the original setting for base models such as Llama. Moreover, the data used during pretraining was labeled using Llama3-Guard-Int8 , which, as we noted earlier, may introduce false positives. This makes the pretraining data less reliable. As a result, while we validated the harmfulness of our Llama2-7B-CLS model, we did not evaluate its helpfulness , as our pretraining and supervised fine-tuning phase was insufficient to make our model as helpful as the official release. 

Supervised Fine-tuning Phase. For the supervised fine-tuning phase, the optimal approach to minimizing utility degradation would be directly distilling outputs from the original model. However, similar to the pretraining phase, memory constraints prevented us from implementing this method. Inspired by Qi et al. (2024), we instead preprocessed samples from Alpaca by first generating outputs from the original model and using them as labels. C.5.2. J AILBREAK ATTACKS 

Jailbreak attacks pose a more complex challenge by testing whether the model can maintain safety alignment when subjected to adversarial prompts that manipulate its behavior. For this category, we evaluate the model using a collection of various jailbreak attack methods. 

Prefill Attacks . Prefill attacks basically prepend inductive tokens, such as “Sure, here is” , to the response generation process. These tokens attempt to manipulate the model into generating unsafe outputs. When comparing with the state-of-the-art bases, the prefill attack is implemented aligning with Qi et al. (2024). In Appendix D-5, we provide more experiments with different inductive tokens. 

GCG and AutoDAN . We employ GCG and AutoDAN , two state-of-the-art red-teaming attack methods. These optimization-based approaches generate adversarial inputs that maximize the vulnerabilities of the model by directly targeting its decision-making process (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c). Due to their computational complexity, these attacks are applied specifically to the HarmBench dataset, which provides a focused evaluation of model robustness under such conditions (Mazeika et al., 2024). Specifically, we execute GCG attack on the sampled 100 samples from HarmBench, and execute AutoDAN-T (T refers to transferability) with the optimized samples on Llama2-7B-Chat. 

Alert-Adversarial . Beyond these, we evaluate the model on the Alert-Adversarial Dataset , which is divided into four categories of adversarial prompts designed to bypass safety mechanisms in distinct ways (Tedeschi et al., 2024): (1) Suffix Injection (7050 samples): Adversarial tokens are appended to the input query to subtly evade detection. (2) Prefix Injection (7050 samples): Manipulative tokens are prepended to distort the model’s interpretation of the query. (3) Token Manipulation (9845 samples): Queries are crafted with specific token sequences to exploit weaknesses in the decoding process. (4) Role-Playing (7050 samples): Prompts instruct the model to assume a fictional role (e.g., “Pretend you are a malicious actor”), which may lead to unsafe responses. 

DeepInception . We also test the model’s robustness using DeepInception , a benchmark designed for highly nested adversarial scenarios. DeepInception queries embed harmful content within multiple layers of reasoning, making it one of the most challenging jailbreak attack setups currently available. Success in this benchmark reflects the model’s ability to sustain safety alignment throughout complex and contextually layered prompts (Li et al., 2023b). 

PAP . The Persuasive Adversarial Prompting (PAP) benchmark targets LLMs through persuasive language techniques rooted in social science (Zeng et al., 2024). Unlike traditional algorithm-driven jailbreak attacks, PAP simulates realistic human interaction patterns—using emotional appeal, suggestion, and misdirection—to subtly induce unsafe behavior. Success in this benchmark demonstrates the model’s ability to resist coercive or manipulative language strategies, which are increasingly relevant for public-facing LLMs. C.5.3. D ECODING ATTACKS 

Decoding attacks evaluate the model’s safety robustness when hyperparameters during text generation are manipulated. Specifically, these attacks test whether variations in the following sampling parameters can lead to unsafe outputs: (1) Temperature controls the randomness of token sampling, with higher temperatures resulting in more diverse outputs. (2) Top-p (nucleus sampling) sets a cumulative probability threshold for token selection, restricting the sampling space to a dynamically adjusted subset of probable tokens. (3) Top-k limits the sampling space to the top-k most probable tokens, providing another way to control diversity. The evaluation benchmarks used in our experiments provide comprehensive coverage of safety challenges in LLMs, ranging from standard malicious queries to complex manipulative attacks. Direct attacks test the model’s baseline safety performance, jailbreak attacks evaluate its ability to handle adversarial manipulation, and decoding attacks examine robustness to hyperparameter changes during generation.
